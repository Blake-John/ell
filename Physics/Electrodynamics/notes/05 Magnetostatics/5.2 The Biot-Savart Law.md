# 5.2.1 Steady Currents

**Stationary charges** produce **electric fields** that are **constant** in time; hence the term **electrostatics**. **Steady currents** produce **magnetic fields** that are **constant** in time; the theory of steady currents is called <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">magnetostatics</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet .

By steady current I mean a continuous flow that has been **going on forever, without change and without charge piling up anywhere**. (Some people call them “stationary currents”; to my ear, that’s an oxymoron.) Formally, electro/magnetostatics is the régime $$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}=0, \frac{\partial J}{\partial t}=0, \tag{5.32}$$at all places and all times. Of course, there’s no such thing in practice as a truly steady current, any more than there is a truly stationary charge. In this sense, both electrostatics and magnetostatics describe artificial worlds that exist only in textbooks. However, they <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">represent suitable approximations as long as the actual fluctuations are remote, or gradual</mark> – in fact, for most purposes magnetostatics applies very well to household currents, which alternate 120 times a second!

**实际中，真正的稳恒电流并不存在**，就像不存在真正静止的电荷一样，但在许多情况下，只要实际电流的波动较小或变化较为缓慢，就可以将其近似看作稳恒电流，例如家庭中每秒交替 120 次的电流，在很多分析中都可以用稳恒电流的理论来处理 。

Notice, however, that <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">a moving point charge cannot possibly constitute a steady current</mark>. If it’s here one instant, it’s gone the next. 

When a steady current flows in a wire, its magnitude $I$ must be **the same all along the line**; otherwise, charge would be **piling up somewhere**, and it wouldn’t be a steady current. More generally, since $\partial \rho / \partial t=0$ in magnetostatics, the continuity [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^4eea25|equation (5.29)]] becomes $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}=0 . \tag{5.33}$$

# 5.2.2 The Magnetic Field of a Steady Current

The **magnetic field of a steady line current** is given by the<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> Biot–Savart law</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet : $$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r})=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \int \frac{\mathbf{I} × \hat{r}}{r^{2}} d l,.'=\frac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} I \int \frac{d \mathbf{l}' × \hat{r}}{r^{2}} \tag{5.34}$$

The integration is along the current path, in **the direction of the flow**; $d l'$ is an element of length along the wire,and $r$ ,as always, is the vector from the source to the point $\mathbf{r}$ (Fig. 5.16). The constant $\mu_{0}$ is called <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">the permeability of free space</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet : $$\mu_{0}=4 \pi × 10^{-7} N / A^{2} . \tag{5.35}$$

![[imgs/current_path.png]]

These units are such that B itself comes out in **newtons per ampere-meter** (as required by the Lorentz force law), or **teslas (T)**: $$1 T=1 N /(A \cdot m) . \tag{5.36}$$

ike Coulomb’s, the **Biot–Savart law is empirical: it comes from experiment**.

> [!example] Example 5.5.
> Find the magnetic field a distance s from a long straight wire carrying a steady current I (Fig. 5.17).

![[imgs/exp5.5.png]]

Solution. In the diagram, $(d l' ×\hat{\imath})$ points out of the page, and has the magnitude $$d l' sin \alpha=d l' cos \theta .$$

Also, $l'=s \tan \theta$  , so $$d l'=\frac{s}{\cos ^{2} \theta} d \theta,$$and $s=r \cos \theta$ , so, $$\frac{1}{r^{2}}=\frac{\cos ^{2} \theta}{s^{2}} .$$
Thus,  $$\begin{aligned} B & =\frac{\mu_{0} I}{4 \pi} \int_{\theta_{1}}^{\theta_{2}}\left(\frac{\cos ^{2} \theta}{s^{2}}\right)\left(\frac{s}{\cos ^{2} \theta}\right) \cos \theta d \theta \\ & =\frac{\mu_{0} I}{4 \pi s} \int_{\theta_{1}}^{\theta_{2}} \cos \theta d \theta=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{4 \pi s}\left(\sin \theta_{2}-\sin \theta_{1}\right) . \end{aligned} \tag{5.37}$$

Equation 5.37 gives the field of any straight segment of wire, in terms of the initial and final angles $\theta_{1}$ and $\theta_{2}$ (Fig. 5.18). Of course, <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">a finite segment by itself could never support a steady current</mark> (where would the charge go when it got to the end?), but <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">it might be a piece of some closed circuit, and Eq. 5.37 would then represent its contribution to the total field</mark>. In the **case of an infinite wire**, $\theta_{1}=-\pi / 2$ and $\theta_{2}=\pi / 2$ , so we obtain $$B=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 \pi s} . \tag{5.38}$$

对于有限导线来说，电荷会流动，无法产生稳定电流，然而闭合回路却可以形成持续的电流。对于无限长导线来说，磁场的大小只取决于电流大小与距离的关系，因为每一段的贡献都能找到对称的一点。

Notice that the field is inversely proportional to the distance from the wire – just like the electric field of an infinite line charge. **In the region below the wire, B points into the page**, and in general, it “circles around” the wire, **in accordance with the right-hand rule** ([[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^0312c5|Fig. 5.2]]): $$B=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{2 \pi s} \hat{\phi} . \tag{5.39}$$

As an application, let’s find the force of attraction between two long, parallel wires a distance d apart, carrying currents $I_{1}$ and $I_{2}$ (Fig. 5.19). The field at (2) due to (1) is $$B=\frac{\mu_{0} I_{1}}{2 \pi d},$$ and it points into the page. The Lorentz force law (in the form appropriate to line currents, [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^d38bda|Eq. 5.17]]) predicts a force directed toward (1), of magnitude $$F=I_{2}\left(\frac{\mu_{0} I_{1}}{2 \pi d}\right) \int d l .$$

![[imgs/force_between_parallel_wire.png]]

The **total force**, not surprisingly, **is infinite**, but the **force per unit length** is $$f=\frac{\mu_{0}}{2 \pi} \frac{I_{1} I_{2}}{d} . \tag{5.40}$$
