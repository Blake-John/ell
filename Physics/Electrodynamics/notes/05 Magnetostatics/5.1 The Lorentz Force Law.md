经典电动力学核心是计算源电荷对测试电荷的作用力。静电学中源电荷静止，而此部分开始研究电荷运动时的受力情况。

# 5.1.1 Magnetic Fields

To give you some sense of what is in store, imagine that I set up the following demonstration: Two wires hang from the ceiling, a few centimeters apart; when I turn on a current, so that it passes up one wire and back down the other, the wires jump apart – they repel one another (Fig. 5.1a). 

当两根导线通以 **相反方向电流时**，它们 **相互排斥**；通以 **相同方向电流时**，则 **相互吸引**。

![[imgs/wire_att_rep.png|400]]

How do we explain this? You might suppose that the battery (or whatever drives the current) is actually charging up the wire, and that the force is simply due to the electrical repulsion of like charges. But this is incorrect. I could <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">hold up a test charge near these wires, and there would be no force on it</mark>,for the <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">wires are in fact electrically neutral</mark>. (It’s true that electrons are flowing down the line – that’s what a current *is* – but there are just as many stationary plus charges as moving minus charges on any given segment.) Moreover, if I hook up my demonstration so as to make the current flow up both wires (Fig. 5.1b), they are found to attract!

这种现象无法用静电学解释，因为导线呈电中性，对附近测试电荷无作用力。由此可知，该力不是静电力，而是一种新的力 —— 磁力，进而引出运动电荷会产生磁场的结论。

Whatever force accounts for the attraction of parallel currents and the repulsion of antiparallel ones is not electrostatic in nature. It is our first encounter with a **magnetic force**. Whereas a stationary charge produces only an electric field E in the space around it,<mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;"> a moving charge generates, in addition, a magnetic field B</mark>.

Now, if you hold up a tiny compass in the vicinity of a current-carrying wire, you quickly discover a very peculiar thing: the field does not point toward the wire, nor away from it, but rather <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">it circles around the wire</mark>. In fact, if **you grab the wire with your right hand – thumb in the direction of the current – your fingers curl around in the direction of the magnetic field** (Fig. 5.2). 

右手螺旋定则

![[imgs/rhr_to_magnet.png|250]] ^0312c5

# 5.1.2 Magnetic Forces

In fact, this combination of directions is just right for a **cross product**: the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">magnetic force on a charge Q , moving with velocity y in a magnetic field B</mark>, is $$\mathbf{F}_{mag }=Q(\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}) . \tag{5.1}$$ ^c14e51

This is known as the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Lorentz force law</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet . In the **presence of both electric and magnetic fields,** the net force on Q would be $$\mathbf{F}=Q[\mathbf{E} +(\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B})] . \tag{5.2}$$

洛伦兹力定律，是电动力学的基本公理，是从大量实验中总结归纳得出的，难以从更基础的理论出发进行严格的数学证明。

> [!example] Example 5.1. (Cyclotron motion) 
> The archetypical motion of a charged particle in a magnetic field is circular, with the **magnetic force providing the centripetal acceleration.** In Fig. 5.4, a uniform magnetic field points into the page; if the charge Q moves counterclockwise, with speed v , around a circle of radius R , the magnetic force points inward, and has a fixed magnitude $Q v B$ – just right to sustain uniform circular motion: $$Q v B=m \frac{v^{2}}{R},\ \text{or}\ p=Q B R, \tag{5.3}$$ 
> 
> where m is the particle’s mass and $p=m v$ is its momentum. Equation 5.3 is known as the **cyclotron formula** because it describes the motion of a particle in a cyclotron – the first of the modern particle accelerators. It also suggests a simple experimental technique for finding the momentum of a charged particle: send it through a region of known magnetic field, and measure the radius of its trajectory. This is in fact the standard means for determining the momenta of elementary particles.
> 
> ![[imgs/exp5.1.png]]
> 
> I assumed that the charge moves in a plane perpendicular to B. **If it starts out with some additional speed $v_{\|}$ parallel to B**, this component of the motion is unaffected by the magnetic field, and the <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">particle moves in a helix </mark>(Fig. 5.5). The radius is still given by Eq. 5.3, but the velocity in question is now the component perpendicular to B: call it $v_{\perp}$ .

> [!example] Example 5.2. (Cycloid motion)
> A more exotic trajectory occurs if we include a uniform electric field, at right angles to the magnetic one. Suppose, for instance, that B points in the x direction, and E in the z direction, as shown in Fig. 5.6. A positive charge is released from the origin; what path will it follow?

![[imgs/exp5.2.png]]

*Solution*. Let’s think it through qualitatively, first. **Initially, the particle is at rest**, so the **magnetic force is zero**, and the <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">electric field accelerates the charge in the z direction</mark>. As it picks up speed, a magnetic force develops which, according to [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^c14e51|Eq. 5.1]], pulls the charge around to the right. The faster it goes, the stronger $F_{mag}$ becomes; eventually, it curves the particle back around toward the y -axis. At this point the charge is moving against the electrical force, so it begins to slow down – the magnetic force then decreases, and the electrical force takes over, bringing the particle to rest at point a , in Fig. 5.6. There the entire process commences anew, carrying the particle over to point b , and so on.

Now let’s do it quantitatively. There being no force in the x direction, the position of the particle at any time t can be described by the vector $r(t)=(0, y(t), z(t))$ ; the velocity is therefore $$v=(0, \dot{y}, \dot{z}),$$

where the dots denote time derivatives. Thus $$v × B=\left|\begin{array}{ccc} \hat{x} & \hat{y} & \hat{z} \\ 0 & \dot{y} & \dot{z} \\ B & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right|=B \dot{z} \hat{y}-B \dot{y} \hat{z},$$

and hence, applying **Newton’s second law**, $$\mathbf{F}=Q(\mathbf{E}+\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B})=Q(E \hat{z}+B \dot{z} \hat{y}-B \dot{y} \hat{z})=m \mathbf{a}=m(\ddot{y} \hat{y}+\ddot{z} \hat{z}) .$$

Or, treating the $\hat{y}$ and $\hat{z}$ components separately, $$Q B \dot{z}=m \ddot{y},\qquad Q E-Q B \dot{y}=m \ddot{z} .$$

For convenience, let $$\omega \equiv \frac{Q B}{m} . \tag{5.4}$$ (This is the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">cyclotron frequency</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet , at which the particle would revolve in the absence of any electric field.) Then the equations of motion take the form $$\ddot{y}=\omega \dot{z},\qquad \ddot{z}=\omega\left(\frac{E}{B}-\dot{y}\right) . \tag{5.5}$$

Their **general solution** is $$\left.\begin{array}{l} y(t)=C_{1} \cos \omega t+C_{2} \sin \omega t+(E / B) t+C_{3}, \\ z(t)=C_{2} \cos \omega t-C_{1} \sin \omega t+C_{4} . \end{array}\right\} \tag{5.6}$$

But the **particle started from rest** ( $\dot{y}(0)=\dot{z}(0)=0$ ), at the origin ( $y(0)=z(0)=0$ ); these four conditions determine the constants $C_{1}$ , $C_{2}$ , $C_{3}$ ,and $C_{4}$ $$y(t)=\frac{E}{\omega B}(\omega t-\sin \omega t),\qquad z(t)=\frac{E}{\omega B}(1-\cos \omega t) . \tag{5.7}$$
> solve the solution #tobesolved 

In this form, the answer is not terribly enlightening, but if we let $$R \equiv \frac{E}{\omega B}, \tag{5.8}$$and **eliminate the sines and cosines** by exploiting the trigonometric identity $\sin ^{2} \omega t+ \cos ^{2} \omega t=1$ , we find that $$(y-R \omega t)^{2}+(z-R)^{2}=R^{2} . \tag{5.9}$$

This is the formula for a circle, of radius R , whose center $(0, R \omega t, R)$ travels in the y direction at a constant speed $$u=\omega R=\frac{E}{B} . \tag{5.10}$$
The particle moves as though it were a spot on the rim of a wheel rolling along the y -axis. The curve generated in this way is called a<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> cycloid</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet . Notice that the overall motion is not in the direction of E , as you might expect, but **perpendicular** to it.

One implication of the Lorentz force law ([[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^c14e51|Eq. 5.1]]) deserves special attention: 

> [!attention] 
> **magnetic forces do no work.** 
> **洛伦兹力不做功**

For if Q moves an amount $d l=v d t$ , the work done is $$d W_{mag }=\mathbf{F}_{mag } \cdot d \mathbf{l}=Q(\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}) \cdot \mathbf{v} d t=0 . \tag{5.11}$$
> $\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}$ 与 $\mathbf{v}$ 相互垂直，点乘得零

# 5.1.3 Currents

The <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">current</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet  in a wire is **the charge per unit time passing a given point**.$$i = \dfrac{dq}{dt}$$

By definition, <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">negative charges moving to the left count the same as positive ones to the right</mark>. This conveniently reflects the physical fact that <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">almost all phenomena involving moving charges depend on the product of charge and velocity – if you reverse the signs of q and v , you get the same answer</mark>, so it doesn’t really matter which you have. (The Lorentz force law is a case in point; the Hall effect (Prob. 5.44) is a notorious exception.)

In practice, it is ordinarily the **negatively charged electrons that do the moving – in the direction opposite to the electric current**. To avoid the petty complications this entails, I shall often pretend it’s the positive charges that move, as in fact everyone assumed they did for a century or so after Benjamin Franklin established his unfortunate convention.6 Current is measured in coulombs per second, or amperes (A): $$1 A=1 C / s . \tag{5.12}$$

A line charge $\lambda$ traveling down a wire at speed $v$ (Fig. 5.8) constitutes a current $$I=\lambda v, \tag{5.13}$$ ^301fdd

![[imgs/linechargedensity.png]]

because a segment of length $v\Delta t$ , carrying charge $\lambda v \Delta t$ , passes point P in a time interval $\Delta t$ . <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Current is actually a vector</mark>: $$\mathbf{I}=\lambda \mathbf{v} . \tag{5.14}$$

电流由电荷产生，所以电流实际上是一个矢量。

A neutral wire, of course, contains as many stationary **positive charges as mobile negative ones**. The **former do not contribute to the current** – the charge density $\lambda$ in [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^301fdd|Eq. 5.13]] **refers only to the moving charges**. In the <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">unusual situation where both types move</mark>, $I=\lambda_{+} v_{-}+\lambda_{-} v_{-}$ 

The magnetic<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> force on a segment of current-carrying wire</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet  is $$\mathbf{F}_{mag }=\int(\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}) d q=\int(\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}) \lambda d l=\int(\mathbf{I} × \mathbf{B}) d l . \tag{5.15}$$
Typically, the current is **constant (in magnitude) along the wire**, and in that case I comes outside the integral: $$\mathbf{F}_{mag }=\mathbf{I} \int(d \mathbf{l} × \mathbf{B}) . \tag{5.17}$$ ^d38bda

> [!example] Example 5.3. 
> A rectangular loop of wire, supporting a mass m , hangs vertically with one end in a uniform magnetic field B , which points into the page in the shaded region of Fig. 5.9. For what current I , in the loop, would the magnetic force **upward exactly balance the gravitational force downward**?

![[imgs/exp5.3_.png]]

Solution. First of all, the current **must circulate clockwise**, in order for ($\mathbf{I}\times \mathbf{B}$) in the horizontal segment to **point upward**. The force is $$F_{mag}=I B a,$$

where a is the width of the loop. (The magnetic forces on the two vertical segments cancel.) For $F_{mag }$ to balance the weight ( $mg$ ), we must therefore have

$$I=\frac{m g}{B a} . \tag{5.18}$$

What happens if we now increase the current? Then the upward magnetic force exceeds the downward force of gravity, and **the loop rises, lifting the weight**. Somebody’s doing work, and it sure looks as though the magnetic force is responsible. Indeed, one is tempted to write $$W_{mag }=F_{mag } h=I B a h, \tag{5.19}$$
where h is the distance the loop rises. But we know that **magnetic forces never do work**. What’s going on here? ^446ae6

磁力永不做功，那为什么这里我们能够计算出功率？

![[imgs/exp5.3.png]]

Well, when the loop **starts to rise**, the <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">charges in the wire are no longer moving horizontally</mark> – their velocity now **acquires an upward component u** , the speed of the loop (Fig. 5.10), **in addition to** the horizontal component w associated with the current ( $I=\lambda w$ ). 

The <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">magnetic force, which is always perpendicular to the velocity, no longer points straight up</mark>, but tilts back. It is **perpendicular to the net displacement** of the charge (which **is in the direction of v** ), and therefore it **does no work on $q$** . It **does have a vertical component** ( $q w B$ ); the net vertical force on all the charge ( $\lambda a$ ) in the upper segment of the loop is $$F_{vert }=\lambda a w B=I B a \tag{5.20}$$

(as before); but now it **also has a horizontal component** ( $quB$ ), which opposes the flow of current. 

<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Whoever is in charge of maintaining that current, therefore, must now push those charges along, against the backward component of the magnetic force</mark>.

真正做功的是 **维持电流的装置**（如电池），**它克服磁场力的水平分量推动电荷流动**，从而使回路上升

The total horizontal force on the top segment is $$F_{horiz }=\lambda a u B . \tag{5.21}$$
In a time $d t$ , the charges move a (horizontal) distance $wdt$ , so the work done by this agency (presumably a battery or a generator) is $$W_{battery }=\lambda a B \int u w d t= \lambda waB\int udt =I B a h,$$

注意在这个过程中，电流的大小不变，因此电荷横向移动的速度 $w$ 为一个常数，可提取到积分号外部，而纵向速度的积分即为线框被举高的距离 $h$ 。

which is precisely what we naively attributed to the magnetic force in [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^446ae6|Eq. 5.19]]. Was work done in this process? Absolutely! **Who did it**?

**The battery**! **What, then, was the role of the magnetic force**? Well, <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">it redirected the horizontal force of the battery into the vertical motion</mark> 。

磁场的作用实际上 **重定向了水平方向上电池的力到竖直方向上** 。

It may help to consider a **mechanical analogy**. Imagine you’re sliding a trunk up a frictionless ramp, by pushing on it horizontally with a mop (Fig. 5.11). 

![[imgs/normal_force_redirect.png]]

The **normal force (N) does no work**, because it is perpendicular to the displacement. But it **does have a vertical component (which in fact is what lifts the trunk), and a (backward) horizontal component (which you have to overcome by pushing on the mop)**.

Who is doing the work here? You are, obviously – and yet your force (which is purely horizontal) is not (at least, not directly) what lifts the box. **The normal force plays the same passive (but crucial) role as the magnetic force** in Ex. 5.3: <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">while doing no work itself, it redirects the efforts of the active agent (you, or the battery, as the case may be), from horizontal to vertical</mark>.

洛伦兹力的例子就如斜坡上的支持力，不做功，但是物体仍然能够上升，正是这种重定向的作用使得水平方向上的力贡献了一个竖直方向上的功。

### Surface Current Density

When **charge flows over a surface**, we describe it by the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">surface current density</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet , **K** , defined as follows: Consider a “ribbon” of infinitesimal width $dl_\perp$ , **running parallel to the flow** (Fig. 5.12). If the current in this ribbon is $d I$ , the surface current density is $$K \equiv \frac{d I}{d l_{\perp}} . \tag{5.22}$$

![[imgs/surface_I.png]]


In words, K is the<mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;"> current per unit width</mark>. In particular, if the (mobile) surface charge density is $\sigma$ and its velocity is $v$ , then $$\mathbf{K}=\sigma \mathbf{v} . \tag{5.23}$$
In general, **K** will vary from point to point over the surface, reflecting variations in $\sigma$ and/or $\mathbf{v}$ . The **magnetic force on the surface current** is $$F_{mag }=\int(\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}) \sigma d a=\int(\mathbf{K} × \mathbf{B}) d a . \tag{5.24}$$
**Caveat**: Just as **E** suffers a **discontinuity at a surface charge**, so <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">B is discontinuous at a surface current</mark>. In Eq. 5.24, you must be careful to use the average field, just as we did in Section 2.5.3. ^5f7661

在静电学中，当存在表面电荷时，电场E会发生不连续性变化 ；类似地，在静磁学里，当有表面电流存在时，磁场B也会出现不连续的情况

### Volume Current Density

When the flow of charge is distributed throughout a three-dimensional region, we describe it by the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">volume current density</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet , **J** , defined as follows: Consider a “tube” of infinitesimal cross section $da$ , running parallel to the flow (Fig. 5.13). If the current in this tube is $d I$ , the volume current density is $$\mathbf{J} \equiv \frac{d \mathbf{I}}{d a_{\perp}} . \tag{5.25}$$ ^527a22

![[imgs/volume_I.png]]

In words, J is the **current per unit area**. If the (mobile) volume charge density is $\rho$ and the velocity is $v$ , then $$\mathbf{J}=\rho \mathbf{v} . \tag{5.26}$$

The **magnetic force on a volume current** is therefore $$\mathbf{F}_{mag }=\int(\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}) \rho d \tau=\int(\mathbf{J} × \mathbf{B}) d \tau . \tag{5.27}$$ ^f4aa75

> [!example] Example 5.4
> (a) A current I is **uniformly distributed over a wire** of circular cross section, with radius a (Fig. 5.14). Find the **volume current density J** 。

![[imgs/exp5.4_1.png]]

Solution. The area (perpendicular to the flow) is $\pi a^{2}$ , so $$J=\frac{I}{\pi a^{2}} .$$

> [!example] Example 5.4
> (b) Suppose the **current density in the wire is proportional to the distance** from the axis, $$J=k s$$ (for some constant k ). Find the **total current in the wire**.

![[imgs/exp5.4_2.png]]

Solution. Because J varies with s , we must integrate [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^527a22|Eq. 5.25]]. The current through the shaded patch (Fig. 5.15) is $J d a_{\perp}$ ,and $d a_{\perp}=s ds d\phi$. So $$I=\int(k s)(s d s d \phi)=2 \pi k \int_{0}^{a} s^{2} d s=\frac{2 \pi k a^{3}}{3} .$$

According to Eq. 5.25, the total current crossing a surface S can be written as $$I=\int_{\mathcal{S}} J d a_{\perp}=\int_{\mathcal{S}} J \cdot d a . \tag{5.28}$$

(The <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">dot product serves neatly to pick out the perpendicular component</mark> of $da.$ ) In particular, the charge per unit time leaving a volume $\mathcal{V}$ is$$\oint_{\mathcal{S}} J \cdot d a=\int_{\mathcal{V}}(\nabla \cdot J) d \tau .$$

Because **charge is conserved**, whatever **flows out through the surface must come at the expense of what remains inside**: $$\int_{\mathcal{V}}(\nabla \cdot J) d \tau=-\frac{d}{d t} \int_{\mathcal{V}} \rho d \tau=-\int_{\mathcal{V}}\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}\right) d \tau .$$ (The minus sign reflects the fact that an **outward flow decreases the charge left in $\mathcal{V}$** .) Since this applies to any volume, we conclude that $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}=-\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} . \tag{5.29}$$
This is the precise **mathematical statement of local charge conservation**; it is called the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">continuity equation</mark> #def/electrodynamics/magnet . ^4eea25


连续性方程是 **电荷守恒的表达式** ，通过体电流密度和电荷密度计算出的电流大小应该一致，因此，对电流密度 $\mathbf{J}$ 的 **散度** 应该等于电荷密度 $\rho$ 的 **负变化率** 

For future reference, let me summarize the “dictionary” we have developed for translating equations into the forms appropriate to point, line, surface, and volume currents: $$\sum_{i=1}^{n}() q_{i} \mathbf{v}_{i} \sim \int_{line }() \mathbf{I} d l \sim \int_{surface }() \mathbf{K} d a \sim \int_{volume }() \mathbf{J} d \tau . \tag{5.30}$$

This correspondence, which is **analogous** to $q \sim \lambda d l \sim \sigma d a \sim \rho d \tau$ for the various charge distributions, generates [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^d38bda|Eqs. 5.15]], [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^5f7661|5.24]], and [[5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^f4aa75|5.27]] from the original Lorentz force law (5.1).
