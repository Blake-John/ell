# 3.2.1 经典镜像问题 The Classic Image Problem

## 1. Problem to be solved

Suppose a point charge q is **held a distance $d$ above an infinite grounded conducting plane** (Fig. 3.10). 

Question: **What is the potential in the region above the plane**? It’s **not just** $(1 / 4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}) q / 2$ ,for q will **induce a certain amount of negative charge on the nearby surface of the conductor**; the total potential is due in part to q directly, and in part to this induced charge. But **how can we possibly calculate the potential, when we don’t know how much charge is induced or how it is distributed**?

- **物理场景**：点电荷 q 位于 **无限大接地导电平面** 上方，垂直距离为 d（图 3.10）。
- **目标**：求解导电平面上方区域（$z > 0$）的电势 $V(x, y, z)$。
- **挑战**：导电平面的 **感应电荷分布未知**，直接积分库仑定律困难。

![[imgs/infinite_conductive_plane.png]]

From a mathematical point of view, our problem is to **solve Poisson’s equation** $\nabla^{2}V = -\dfrac{\rho}{\varepsilon_{0}}$ in the region $z>0$ , with a single point charge q at $(0,0, d)$ , subject to the following boundary conditions:

1. $V=0$ when $z=0$ (since the **conducting plane is grounded**), 
2. $V \to 0$ far from the charge (that is, for $x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2} \gg d^{2})$ .

实际上我们需要在 $z > 0$ 的区域上解 **泊松方程** 来求 $V$ 。

## 2. Trick

我们先看另一个简单的，与之不同的问题。

This new configuration consists of **two point charges**, $+q$ at $(0, 0, d)$ and $−q$ at $(0, 0, −d)$, and **no conducting plane** (Fig. 3.11). For this configuration, I can easily write down the potential:

$$V(x, y, z)=\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}\left[\frac{q}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}+(z-d)^{2}}}-\frac{q}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}+(z+d)^{2}}}\right] \tag{3.9}$$ ^1d922e

![[imgs/two_point_no_plane.png]]

(The denominators represent the distances from $(x, y, z)$ to the charges $+q$ and $-q$ , respectively.) It follows that

1. $V=0$ when $z=0$ 
2. $V \to 0$ for $x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2} \gg d^{2})$ 

and the only charge in the region $z > 0$ is the point charge $+q$ at $(0, 0, d)$ . But these are **precisely the conditions of the original problem** !

第二个问题在 $z > 0$ 的区域上满足的 **电荷分布与前一个一致**，其 **边界条件也与前一个问题一致**。

The second configuration happens to **produce exactly the same potential as the first configuration**, in the **“upper” region** $z ≥0$ . (The **“lower” region**, $z<0$ , is **completely different**, but who cares? The upper part is all we need.) 

**Conclusion**: The potential of a point charge above an infinite grounded conductor is given by [[3.2 The Method of Images#^1d922e|Eq. 3.9]] , for $z ≥0$ .

因此，我们知道第二个构型的电势在 $z > 0$ 的区域上与第一个构型一致，并且由 Eq 3.9 给出。

Notice the **crucial role played by the uniqueness theorem** in this argument: **without it, no one would believe this solution, since it was obtained for a completely different charge distribution**. But the uniqueness theorem certifies it: if it **satisfies Poisson’s equation** in the region of interest, and assumes the **correct value at the boundaries**, then it must be right.

虽然这个结果并不是那么容易让人信服，但是它满足泊松方程，并且在边界上指出了正确的值，因此这个答案就是正确的，这就是唯一性定理给出的答案。

# 3.2.2 表面感应电荷 (Induced Surface Charge)

Now that we know the potential, it is **a straightforward matter to compute the surface charge $\sigma$ induced on the conductor**. According to [[Eq. 2.49]], $$\sigma=-\varepsilon_{0} \frac{\partial V}{\partial n},$$where $\partial V / \partial n$ is the **normal derivative of $V$ at the surface**. In this case the normal direction is the z direction, so $$\sigma=-\left.\varepsilon_{0} \frac{\partial V}{\partial z}\right|_{z=0} .$$
我们可以通过 **导体表面的电势** 来确定 **表面的电荷分布** 

From [[3.2 The Method of Images#^1d922e|Eq. 3.9]] , $$\frac{\partial V}{\partial z}=\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}\left\{\frac{-q(z-d)}{\left[x^{2}+y^{2}+(z-d)^{2}\right]^{3 / 2}}+\frac{q(z+d)}{\left[x^{2}+y^{2}+(z+d)^{2}\right]^{3 / 2}}\right\}$$$$\left.\dfrac{\partial V}{\partial z}\right|_{z = 0} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0}} \left( \dfrac{-q(0 - d)}{[x^{2} + y^{2} + (0 - d)^{2}]^{3/2}} + \dfrac{q(0 + d)}{[x^{2} + y^{2} + (0 + d)^{2}]^{3/2}}\right) = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{2qd}{(x^{2} + y^{2} + d^{2})^{3/2}}$$
so $$\sigma(x, y)=\frac{-q d}{2 \pi\left(x^{2}+y^{2}+d^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}} \tag{3.10}$$As **expected, the induced charge is negative** (assuming q is positive) and greatest at $x=y=0$

由 [[3.2 The Method of Images#^1d922e|Eq 3.9]] 得出的电势在电荷量 **极性** 上是符合预期的。

While we’re at it, let’s compute the **total induced charge** $$Q=\int \sigma d a .$$This integral, over the $x y$ plane, could be done in Cartesian coordinates, with $d a=d x d y$ , but it’s a little easier to use polar coordinates $(r, \phi)$ , with $r^{2}=x^{2}+y^{2}$ and $d a=r d r d \phi$ . Then $$\sigma(r)=\frac{-q d}{2 \pi\left(r^{2}+d^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}},$$and $$Q=\int_{0}^{2 \pi} \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{-q d}{2 \pi\left(r^{2}+d^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}} r d r d \phi=\left.\frac{q d}{\sqrt{r^{2}+d^{2}}}\right|_{0} ^{\infty}=-q . \tag{3.11}$$
The total charge induced on the plane is $-q$ , as (with benefit of hindsight) you can perhaps convince yourself it had to be.

我们通过计算总的感应电荷，可以得到，面上的感应电荷的总量为 $-q$ ，与我们放置负电荷的量一致。

> [!attention] 
> 这里需要注意的是，**若平面有限**，**边缘效应** 会导致 **电荷分布不均匀**，镜像法不再适用

# 3.2.3 力和能量 (Force and Energy)

The charge $q$ is attracted toward the plane, because of the **negative induced charge**. Let’s calculate the force of attraction. Since the potential in the vicinity of q is the same as in the analog problem (the one with $+q$ and $-q$ but no conductor), so also is the field and, therefore, the force: $$F=-\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \frac{q^{2}}{(2 d)^{2}} \hat{z} . \tag{3.12}$$ ^c017a2

导体面上的电荷受到感应出来的负电荷的吸引，这个力是由于电场强度产生的。因为使用镜像法得到的场强与实际场强一致，因此受到的吸引力 **等效为镜像法模型中** 电荷受到的吸引力。

> [!attention] 
> *Beware*: It is easy to get carried away, and assume that everything is the same in the two problems. **Energy, however, is not the same.** 

With the two point charges and no conductor, [[Eq. 2.42]] gives $$W=-\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \frac{q^{2}}{2 d} . \tag{3.13}$$
But for a **single charge and conducting plane**, the energy is **half of this** : $$W=-\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \frac{q^{2}}{4 d} . \tag{3.14}$$

实际上，该模型的能量为镜像法模型的一半，为什么？

Why half? Think of the **energy as stored in the fields** ([[Eq. 2.45]]): $$W=\frac{\varepsilon_{0}}{2} \int E^{2} d \tau .$$

In the two-charge analog (Fig. 3.11), **both the upper region ( $(z>0)$ and the lower region ( $(z<0)$ ) contribute** – and by symmetry they **contribute equally**. But in the original configuration (Fig. 3.10), **only the upper region contains a nonezero field** , and hence the energy is half as great.

能量存储在场强中，在镜像法中，场强充满了两个电荷之间的空间，然而，在原构型中，由于 **静电屏蔽**，只有在 **导体平面上部的区域存在场强**，相比之下减少了一半的场强，因此能量也是镜像法的一半。

Of course, one could also determine the energy by **calculating the work it takes to bring $q$ in from infinity**. The force required (to oppose the electrical force in [[3.2 The Method of Images#^c017a2|Eq. 3.12]]) is $(1 / 4 \pi \epsilon_{0})(q^{2} / 4 z^{2}) \hat{z}$ , so $$\begin{aligned} W=\int_{\infty}^{d} F \cdot d l & =\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \int_{\infty}^{d} \frac{q^{2}}{4 z^{2}} d z \\ & =\left.\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}}\left(-\frac{q^{2}}{4 z}\right)\right|_{\infty} ^{d}=-\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{q^{2}}{4 d} . \end{aligned}$$

As I move q toward the conductor, I **do work only on $q$** . It is true that **induced charge is moving in over the conductor, but this costs me nothing, since the whole conductor is at potential zero**. By contrast, if I **simultaneously bring in two point charges (with no conductor), I do work on both of them, and the total is (again) twice as great**.

如果我将该电荷从无穷远移动到 $d$ 处，那么我 **只需要对该电荷做功**，这是因为我们视该导体面为 **等势面** ，**感应电荷在其上的运动不消耗能量**。在镜像法中，如果想要在相同的电场中做功，必须 **同时移动两个电荷** 才能保证每一时刻的力和场强与原构型相同，因此，我们需要的功为原构型的两倍，因此能量为两倍。 

# 3.2.4 其他镜像问题 (Other Image Problems)

The method just described is **not limited to a single point charge**; any <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">stationary charge distribution near a grounded conducting plane</mark> can be treated in the same way, by introducing its mirror image – hence the name method of images. (<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Remember that the image charges have the opposite sign</mark>; this is what guarantees that the $x y$ plane will be at potential zero.) 

用于处理任何静止电荷分布靠近接地导电平面的情况。并且，镜像电荷的 **符号与原电荷相反**，以此确保导电平面的电势为零

> [!example] Example 3.2. 
> A point charge $q$ is situated a distance a from the **center of a grounded conducting sphere** of radius $R<a$ (Fig. 3.12). Find the **potential outside the sphere**.

![[imgs/exp3.2.png]]

*Solution*. Examine the **completely different configuration**, consisting of the point charge $q$ together with another point charge $$q'=-\frac{R}{a} q, \tag{3.15}$$placed a distance $$b=\frac{R^{2}}{a} \tag{3.16}$$to the right of the center of the sphere (Fig. 3.13). No conductor now – just the two point charges. The potential of this configuration is $$V(r)=\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}\left(\frac{q}{r}+\frac{q'}{r'}\right),$$
电势为 **标量** ，可以直接叠加。

where $r$ and $r'$ are the distances from $q$ and $q'$ , respectively. Now, it happens (see Prob. 3.8) that this **potential vanishes at all points on the sphere** ( $r=R$ ), and therefore **fits the boundary conditions** for our original problem, in the exterior region.

*Conclusion*: **Equation 3.17 is the potential of a point charge near a grounded conducting sphere**.

(Notice that $b$ is less than $R$ , so the “image” charge $q'$ is safely inside the sphere – **you cannot put image charges in the region where you are calculating $V$** ; that would change $ρ$ , and you’d be solving Poisson’s equation with the wrong source.) In particular, the force of attraction between the charge and the sphere is $$F=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{q q'}{(a-b)^{2}}=-\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_{0}} \frac{q^{2} R a}{\left(a^{2}-R^{2}\right)^{2}} . (3.18)$$
