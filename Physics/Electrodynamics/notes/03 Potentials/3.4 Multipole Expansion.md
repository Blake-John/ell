# 3.4.1 Approximate Potentials at Large Distances

If you are very **far away from a localized charge distribution**, it **“looks” like a point charge**, and the potential is, to **good approximation**, $(1 / 4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}) Q / r$ ,

> [!example]  Example 3.10. 
> A (physical) electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges ( $\pm q$ ) separated by a distance d . Find the **approximate potential** at points far from the dipole.

![[imgs/exp3.10.png]]

**Solution**. Let $r_{-}$ be the distance from $-q$ and $r_{+}$ the distance from $+q$ (Fig. 3.26). Then $$V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}\left(\dfrac{q}{r_{+}}-\dfrac{q}{r_{-}}\right),$$ and (from the law of cosines) $$r_{ \pm}^{2}=r^{2}+(d / 2)^{2} \mp r d \cos \theta=r^{2}\left(1 \mp \dfrac{d}{r} \cos \theta+\dfrac{d^{2}}{4 r^{2}}\right) .$$
We’re interested in the distant zone, where $r \gg d$ , so the **third term is negligible**, and the **binomial expansion yields** $$\dfrac{1}{r_{ \pm}} \approx \dfrac{1}{r}\left(1 \mp \dfrac{d}{r} \cos \theta\right)^{-1 / 2} \approx \dfrac{1}{r}\left(1 \pm \dfrac{d}{2 r} \cos \theta\right) .$$   
$$\dfrac{1}{r_{+}}-\dfrac{1}{r_{-}} \approx \dfrac{d}{r^{2}} \cos \theta,$$   
$$V(r) \approx \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{q d \cos \theta}{r^{2}} . \tag{3.90}$$ ^c8648c

The potential of a dipole goes like $1/r^{2}$ at large $r$ ; as we might have anticipated, it falls off more rapidly than the potential of a point charge. If we put together a pair of equal and opposite dipoles to make a **quadrupole**, the potential goes like $1 / r^{3}$ ; for back-to-back quadrupoles (an **octopole**), it goes like $1/r^{4}$ ; and so on. this hierarchy; for completeness I have included the electric **monopole** (point charge), whose potential, of course, goes like $1/r$

Example 3.10 involves a specific charge configuration. **I propose now to develop a systematic expansion for the potential of any localized charge distribution**, in powers of $1/r$ . Figure 3.28 defines the relevant variables; the potential at $r$ is given by $$V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \int \dfrac{1}{r} \rho\left(r'\right) d \tau' . \tag{3.91}$$
在这里，将图中原点到点 $P$ 的位移记为 $r_{0}$ ，将原点到 $d\tau'$ 的位移记为 $r'$ ，将 $d\tau'$ 到点 $P$ 的位移记为 $r$ 。
Using the **law of cosines**, $$r^{2}=r_{0}^{2}+\left(r'\right)^{2}-2 r_{0} r' \cos \alpha=r_{0}^{2}\left[1+\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}\right)^{2}-2\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}\right) \cos \alpha\right],$$where $\alpha$ is the angle between $r_{0}$ and $r'$ . Thus $$r=r_{0} \sqrt{1+\varepsilon}, \tag{3.92}$$ with $$\varepsilon \equiv\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}\right)\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}-2 \cos \alpha\right) .$$ ^889388

For points well outside the charge distribution, $\varepsilon$ is much less than 1, and this invites a **binomial expansion** : $$\dfrac{1}{r}=\dfrac{1}{r_{0}}(1+\varepsilon)^{-1 / 2}=\dfrac{1}{r_{0}}\left(1-\dfrac{1}{2} \varepsilon+\dfrac{3}{8} \varepsilon^{2}-\dfrac{5}{16} \varepsilon^{3}+\cdots\right), \tag{3.93}$$or, in terms of r , $r'$ ,and α , $$\begin{aligned} \dfrac{1}{r}=\dfrac{1}{r_{0}}[ & \left.1-\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}\right)\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}-2 \cos \alpha\right)+\dfrac{3}{8}\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}\right)^{2}\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}-2 \cos \alpha\right)^{2}\right. \\ & \left.-\dfrac{5}{16}\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}\right)^{3}\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}-2 \cos \alpha\right)^{3}+\cdots\right] \\ = & \dfrac{1}{r}\left[1+\left(\dfrac{r'}{r}\right)(\cos \alpha)+\left(\dfrac{r'}{r}\right)^{2}\left(\dfrac{3 \cos ^{2} \alpha-1}{2}\right)\right. \\ & \left.+\left(\dfrac{r'}{r}\right)^{3}\left(\dfrac{5 \cos ^{3} \alpha-3 \cos \alpha}{2}\right)+\cdots\right] . \end{aligned}$$
In the last step, I have **collected together like powers** of $( r' / r)$ ; surprisingly, their coefficients (the terms in parentheses) are [[3.3 Separation of Variables#^29fa00|Legendre polynomials]] ! The remarkable result is that $$\dfrac{1}{r}=\dfrac{1}{r_{0}} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\left(\dfrac{r'}{r_{0}}\right)^{n} P_{n}(cos \alpha) . \tag{3.94}$$
Substituting this back into [[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^889388|Eq. 3.91]], and noting that r is a constant, as far as the integration is concerned, I concludemn that $$V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{r^{(n+1)}} \int\left(r'\right)^{n} P_{n}(cos \alpha) \rho\left(r'\right) d \tau', \tag{3.95}$$ or, more explicitly, $$\begin{aligned} V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}[ & \dfrac{1}{r} \int \rho\left(r'\right) d \tau'+\dfrac{1}{r^{2}} \int r' cos \alpha \rho\left(r'\right) d \tau' \\ & \left.+\dfrac{1}{r^{3}} \int\left(r'\right)^{2}\left(\dfrac{3}{2} cos ^{2} \alpha-\dfrac{1}{2}\right) \rho\left(r'\right) d \tau'+\cdots\right] . \end{aligned} \tag{3.96}$$This is the desired result – the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">multipole expansion</mark> #def/electrodynamics/potentials  of $V$ in powers of $1/r$ . **The first term ( $n=0$ ) is the monopole contribution** (it goes like $1/r$ ); **the second ( $n=1$ ) is the dipole** (it goes like $1/r^{2}$ ); **the third is quadrupole**; **the fourth octopole**; and so on. Remember that $\alpha$ is the angle between $r_{0}$ and $r'$ , so the integrals depend on the direction to the field point. If you are interested in the potential along the $z'$ -axis (or – putting it the other way around – if you orient your $r'$ coordinates so the $z'$ -axis lies along r), then $\alpha$ is the usual polar angle $\theta'$ ^f87d81

在这个展开式中，每一个幂次都代表了一种构型对电势的贡献，$n = 0$ 为单极子，即点电荷； $n = 1$ 为偶极子； $n = 2$ 为四极子； $n = 3$ 为八极子。

As it stands, Eq. 3.95 is exact, but it is useful primarily as an approximation scheme: **the lowest nonzero term in the expansion provides the approximate potential at large $\mathbf{r}$** , and the **successive terms tell us how to improve the approximation** if greater precision is required.

低阶项对远处的电势有着较好的近似，总的幂次越高，近似效果越好。

# 3.4.2 The Monopole and Dipole Terms

Ordinarily, the **multipole expansion is dominated (at large r ) by the monopole term**: $$V_{mon }(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{Q}{r}, \tag{3.97}$$where $Q=\int \rho d \tau$ is **the total charge of the configuration**. This is just what we expect for the approximate potential at large distances from the charge. For a point charge at the origin, $V_{mon }$ is the exact potential, not merely a first approximation at large $\mathbf{r}$ ; in this case, all the higher multipoles vanish.

当构型为点电荷的时候，所有高阶项都消失，只留下第一项 $n = 0$ ，且此时不再是近似，而是准确结果。

If the **total charge is zero, the dominant term in the potential will be the dipole** (unless, of course, it also vanishes): $$V_{dip }(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{1}{r^{2}} \int r' \cos \alpha \rho\left(r'\right) d \tau' .$$
Since $\alpha$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}'$ and $\mathbf{r}$ ([[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^decc8b|Fig. 3.28]]), $$r' \cos \alpha=\hat{r} \cdot \mathbf{r}',$$and the dipole potential can be written more succinctly: $$V_{dip }(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{1}{r^{2}} \hat{r} \cdot \int \mathbf{r}' \rho\left(\mathbf{r}'\right) d \tau' .$$
This integral (which does not depend on r ) is called<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> the dipole moment of the distribution</mark> #def/electrodynamics/potentials : $$\mathbf{p} \equiv \int \mathbf{r}' \rho\left(\mathbf{r}'\right) d \tau', \tag{3.98}$$and the dipole contribution to the potential simplifies to $$V_{dip }(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{\mathbf{p} \cdot \hat{r}}{r^{2}} \tag{3.99}$$ ^1f8170

当总电荷为0的时候，相当于一个偶极子，电势主要靠偶极子项 $n = 1$ 来贡献（除非没有电荷）。

The dipole moment is determined by the geometry (size, shape, and density) of the charge distribution. Equation 3.98 translates in the usual way ([[../02 Electrostatics/2.1 The Electric Field#2.1.4 Continuous Charge Distributions|Section 2.1.4]]) for point, line, and surface charges. Thus, the dipole moment of a collection of point charges is $$\mathbf{p}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} q_{i} \mathbf{r}_{i}' . \tag{3.100}$$

For a **physical dipole** (**equal and opposite charges**, $\pm q$ ), $$\mathbf{p}=q \mathbf{r}_{+}'-q \mathbf{r}_{-}'=q\left(\mathbf{r}_{+}'-\mathbf{r}_{-}'\right)=q \mathbf{d}, \tag{3.101}$$where $\mathbf{d}$ is the **vector from the negative charge to the positive one** (Fig. 3.29).

![[imgs/physical_dipole.png]]

**Is this consistent with what we got in [[3.4 Multipole Expansion#3.4.1 Approximate Potentials at Large Distances|Ex. 3.10]]**? **Yes**: if you put Eq. 3.101 into [[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^1f8170|Eq. 3.99]], you recover [[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^c8648c|Eq. 3.90]]. Notice, however, that this **is only the approximate potential of the physical dipole** – evidently there are higher multipole contributions. Of course, as you go farther and farther away, $V_{dip }$ becomes a better and better approximation, **since the higher terms die off more rapidly with increasing $\mathbf{r}$** . By the same token, at a fixed $\mathbf{r}$ the dipole approximation improves as you shrink the separation $\mathbf{d}$ . **To construct a perfect (ideal, pure, point) dipole whose potential is given exactly by [[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^1f8170|Eq. 3.99]]**, you’d have to **let $\mathbf{d}$ approach zero.** Unfortunately, you then lose the dipole term too, unless you simultaneously arrange for $q$ to go to infinity. A physical dipole becomes a pure dipole, then, in the rather artificial limit $d \to 0$ , $q \to \infty$ , with the product $q d,.=p$ held fixed. When someone uses the word “dipole,”you can’t always tell whether they mean a physical dipole (with finite separation between the charges) or an ideal (point) dipole. If in doubt, assume that d is small enough (compared to $\mathbf{r}$ ) that you can safely apply Eq. 3.99.

**Dipole moments are vectors**, and they add accordingly: if you have two dipoles, $\mathbf{p}_{1}$ and $\mathbf{p}_{2}$ , the total dipole moment is $\mathbf{p}_{1}+\mathbf{p}_{2}$ . For instance, with four charges at the corners of a square, as shown in Fig. 3.30, the **net dipole moment is zero**. You can see this by combining the charges in pairs (vertically, $\downarrow+\uparrow=0$ , or horizontally, $\to +\leftarrow=0$ ) or by adding up the four contributions individually, using Eq. 3.100. This is a quadrupole, as I indicated earlier, and its potential is dominated by the quadrupole term in the multipole expansion.

# 3.4.3 Origin of Coordinates in Multipole Expansions

I mentioned earlier that a point charge at the origin constitutes a “pure” monopole. If it is not at the origin, it’s no longer a pure monopole. For instance, the charge in Fig. 3.32 has a dipole moment $\mathbf{p}=q d \hat{y}$ , and a corresponding dipole term in its potential. The monopole potential $(1 / 4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}) q / r$ is not quite correct for this configuration; rather, the exact potential is $(1 / 4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}) q / r'$  . The multipole expansion is, remember, a series in inverse powers of r (the distance from the origin), and when we expand $1 / r'$ ,we get all powers, not just the first.

![[imgs/3.32.png]]

So **moving the origin** (or, what amounts to the same thing, moving the charge) can radically alter a multipole expansion. The **monopole moment** $Q$ does not change, since the total charge is obviously independent of the coordinate system. (In Fig. 3.32, the monopole term was unaffected when we moved $q$ away from the origin – it’s just that it was no longer the whole story: a dipole term – and for that matter all higher poles – appeared as well.) Ordinarily, the **dipole moment does change when you shift the origin**, but there is an important exception: **if the total charge is zero, then the dipole moment is independent of the choice of origin**. For suppose we **displace the origin by an amount** $a$ (Fig. 3.33). The new dipole moment is then$$\begin{aligned} \overline{\mathbf{p}}=\int \overline{\mathbf{r}}' \rho\left(\mathbf{r}'\right) d \tau' & =\int\left(\mathbf{r}'-\mathbf{a}\right) \rho\left(\mathbf{r}'\right) d \tau' \\ & =\int \mathbf{r}' \rho\left(\mathbf{r}'\right) d \tau'-\mathbf{a} \int \rho\left(\mathbf{r}'\right) d \tau'=\mathbf{p}-Q \mathbf{a} . \end{aligned}$$
In particular, if $Q=0$ then $\overline{\mathbf{p}}=\mathbf{p}$ . So if someone asks for the dipole moment in Fig. 3.34a, you can answer with confidence “$q\mathbf{d}$,” but if you’re asked for the dipole moment in Fig. 3.34b, the appropriate response would be “With respect to what origin?”

![[imgs/3.33.png]]![[imgs/3.34.png]]

对于单极子而言，其单极矩为总电荷（[[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^f87d81|式 3.95]] 中勒让德多项式的第一项为 1，则计算结果为总电荷），因此移动坐标原点/移动电荷对单极矩没有影响。而对于多极子而言，由于坐标进行移动，通过积分式 [[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^1f8170|3.98]] 得到的偶极矩会发生变化，因此与坐标有关。但是也有特例，当总电荷为0时，变化的项为0，则偶极矩不变。对于更高阶的极子而言，其极矩往往与坐标原点的选取有关。

# 3.4.4 The Electric Field of a Dipole

So far we have worked only with potentials. Now I would like to **calculate the electric field of a (perfect) dipole**. If we choose coordinates so that $\mathbf{p}$ is at the origin and points in the $z$ direction (Fig. 3.36), then the potential at $r , \theta$ is ([[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^1f8170|Eq. 3.99]]) $$V_{dip }(r, \theta)=\dfrac{\hat{r} \cdot \mathbf{p}}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r^{2}}=\dfrac{p cos \theta}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r^{2}} . \tag{3.102}$$
To get the field, we **take the negative gradient** of $V$ $$\begin{array}{l}
E_{r}=-\dfrac{\partial V}{\partial r}=\dfrac{2 p \cos \theta}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r^{3}}, \\
E_{\theta}=-\dfrac{1}{r} \dfrac{\partial V}{\partial \theta}=\dfrac{p \sin \theta}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r^{3}}, \\
E_{\phi}=-\dfrac{1}{r sin \theta} \dfrac{\partial V}{\partial \phi}=0 .
\end{array}$$
Thus   
$$E_{dip }(r, \theta)=\dfrac{p}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r^{3}}(2 \cos \theta \hat{\mathbf{r}}+\sin \theta \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}) . \tag{3.103}$$
This formula makes explicit reference to a particular coordinate system (spherical) and assumes a particular orientation for $\mathbf{p}$ (along $z$). It can be recast in a coordinate-free form, analogous to the potential in Eq. 3.99 – see Prob. 3.38. ^6d73b0

通过对电势取负梯度，我们可以直接得到电场在球坐标系下的各个分量。球坐标系下的梯度为 ![[../01 Vector Analysis/1.4 Curvilinear Coordinates#Gradients|1.4 Curvilinear Coordinates]]

Notice that the dipole field **falls off as the inverse cube of $\mathbf{r}$** ; the **monopole field** $(Q / 4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r^{2}) \hat{r}$ **goes as the inverse square**, of course. Quadrupole fields go like $1 / r^{4}$ , octopole like $1 / r^{5}$ , and so on. (This reflects the fact that monopole potentials fall off like $1/r$ , dipole like $1/r2$ , quadrupole like $1 / r^{3}$ , and so on – **the gradient introduces another factor of $1/r$** )

对于电场而言，其衰减幅度总是比电势更高一阶。

Figure 3.37a shows the field lines of a “pure” dipole ([[3.4 Multipole Expansion#^6d73b0|Eq. 3.103]]). For comparison, I have also sketched the field lines for a “physical” dipole, in Fig. 3.37b. Notice how similar the two pictures become if you blot out the central region; up close, however, they are entirely different. Only for points $r \gg d$ does Eq. 3.103 represent a valid approximation to the field of a physical dipole. As I mentioned earlier, this régime can be reached either by going to large r or by squeezing the charges very close together.
