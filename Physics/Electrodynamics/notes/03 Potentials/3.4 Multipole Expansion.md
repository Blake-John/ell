# 3.4.1 Approximate Potentials at Large Distances

If you are very **far away from a localized charge distribution**, it **“looks” like a point charge**, and the potential is, to **good approximation**, $(1 / 4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}) Q / r$ ,

> [!example] Example 3.10. 
> A (physical) electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges ( $\pm q$ ) separated by a distance d . Find the **approximate potential** at points far from the dipole.

Solution. Let $r_{-}$ be the distance from $-q$ and $r_{+}$ the distance from $+q$ (Fig. 3.26). Then $$V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}\left(\dfrac{q}{r_{+}}-\dfrac{q}{r_{-}}\right),$$ and (from the law of cosines) $$r_{ \pm}^{2}=r^{2}+(d / 2)^{2} \mp r d \cos \theta=r^{2}\left(1 \mp \dfrac{d}{r} \cos \theta+\dfrac{d^{2}}{4 r^{2}}\right) .$$
We’re interested in the distant zone, where $r \gg d$ , so the **third term is negligible**, and the binomial expansion yields $$\dfrac{1}{r_{ \pm}} \approx \dfrac{1}{r}\left(1 \mp \dfrac{d}{r} \cos \theta\right)^{-1 / 2} \approx \dfrac{1}{r}\left(1 \pm \dfrac{d}{2 r} \cos \theta\right) .$$   
$$\dfrac{1}{r_{+}}-\dfrac{1}{r_{-}} \approx \dfrac{d}{r^{2}} \cos \theta,$$   
$$V(r) \approx \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{q d \cos \theta}{r^{2}} . \tag{3.90}$$

The potential of a dipole goes like $1/r^{2}$ at large $r$ ; as we might have anticipated, it falls off more rapidly than the potential of a point charge. If we put together a pair of equal and opposite dipoles to make a **quadrupole**, the potential goes like $1 / r^{3}$ ; for back-to-back quadrupoles (an **octopole**), it goes like $1/r^{4}$ ; and so on.
