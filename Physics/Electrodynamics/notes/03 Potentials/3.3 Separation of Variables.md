# 3.3.0 Introduction

In this section we **attack Laplace’s equation directly,** using <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">the method of separation of variables</mark>, which is the physicist’s favorite tool for solving partial differential equations. The **method is applicable in circumstances where the potential ( $V$ ) or the charge density ( $\sigma$ ) is specified on the boundaries of some region**, and we are asked to find the potential in the interior. 

The basic strategy is very simple: <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">we look for solutions that are products of functions</mark>, each of which **depends on only one of the coordinates**. The algebraic details, however, can be formidable, so I’m going to develop the method through a sequence of examples. We’ll start with Cartesian coordinates and then do spherical coordinates (I’ll leave the cylindrical case for you to tackle on your own, in Prob. 3.26).

# 3.3.1 Cartesian Coordinates

> [!example] Example 3.3. 
> Two **infinite grounded metal plates** lie parallel to the $x z$ plane, one at $y=0$ , the other at $y=a$ (Fig. 3.17). The left end, at $x=0$ , is **closed off with an infinite strip insulated from the two plates**, and maintained at a specified potential $V_{0}(y)$ . Find the potential inside this “slot.”

![[imgs/exp3.3.png|500]]

*Solution*. The **configuration is independent of z** , so this is really a **twodimensional problem**. In mathematical terms, we must solve Laplace’s equation, $$\frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial y^{2}}=0, \tag{3.20}$$subject to the **boundary conditions** $$\left.\begin{array}{ll} (i) V=0 & \text{when}\ y=0, \\ (ii) V=0 & \text{when}\ y=a, \\ (iii) V=V_{0}(y) & \text{when}\ x=0, \\ (iv) V \to 0 & \text{as}\ x \to \infty . \end{array}\right\} \tag{3.21}$$ ^211eff

(The latter, although not explicitly stated in the problem, is necessary on physical grounds: **as you get farther and farther away from the “hot” strip at $x=0$ , the potential should drop to zero**.) Since the potential is specified on all boundaries, the answer is uniquely determined.

在物理学中，我们通常会存在无穷远处的电势为0的条件。

The first step is to look for solutions in the form of products: $$V(x, y)=X(x) Y(y) . \tag{3.22}$$

Anyway, putting Eq. 3.22 into Eq. 3.20, we obtain $$Y \frac{d^{2} X}{d x^{2}}+X \frac{d^{2} Y}{d y^{2}}=0 .$$

The next step is to <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">“separate the variables”</mark> (that is, collect all the x dependence into one term and all the y -dependence into another). Typically, this is accomplished by dividing through by V $$\frac{1}{X} \frac{d^{2} X}{d x^{2}}+\frac{1}{Y} \frac{d^{2} Y}{d y^{2}}=0 . \tag{3.23}$$

Here the <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">first term depends only on x and the second only on y</mark> ; in other words, we have an **equation of the form** $$f(x)+g(y)=0 . \tag{3.24}$$

Now, there’s only <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">one way this could possibly be true: f and g must both be constant</mark>. For if $f(x)$ changed, **as you vary x , then by holding y fixed and fiddling with x , the sum $f(x)+g(y)$ would change**, in violation of Eq. 3.24, which says it’s always zero. (That’s a simple but somehow rather elusive argument; don’t accept it without due thought, because the whole method rides on it.)

当且仅当 $f, g$ 都为常数时才能满足两函数之和恒为0.

It follows from Eq. 3.23, then, that $$\frac{1}{X} \frac{d^{2} X}{d x^{2}}=C_{1} \qquad and \qquad \frac{1}{Y} \frac{d^{2} Y}{d y^{2}}=C_{2}, \qquad with\quad C_{1}+C_{2}=0 . \tag{3.25}$$

One of these constants is **positive**, the other **negative** (or **perhaps both are zero**). In general, one must investigate all the possibilities; however, in this particular problem we need $C_{1}$ positive (call it $k^{2}$ ) and $C_{2}$ negative, for reasons that will appear in a moment. Thus $$\frac{d^{2} X}{d x^{2}}=k^{2} X, \frac{d^{2} Y}{d y^{2}}=-k^{2} Y . \tag{3.26}$$

Notice what has happened: **a partial differential equation** (3.20) has been **converted into two ordinary differential equations** (3.26). The advantage of this is obvious – ordinary differential equations are a lot easier to solve. Indeed, $$X(x)=A e^{k x}+B e^{-k x}, Y(y)=C sin k y+D cos k y,$$so $$V(x, y)=\left(A e^{k x}+B e^{-k x}\right)(C sin k y+D cos k y) . \tag{3.27}$$

This is the<mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;"> appropriate separable solution to Laplace’s equation</mark>; it <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">remains to impose the boundary conditions,</mark> and see what they tell us about the constants. To begin at the end, **[[3.3 Separation of Variables#^211eff|condition (iv)]] requires that A equal zero**.Absorbing B into c and D , we are left with
  
$$V(x, y)=e^{-k x}(C sin k y+D cos k y) .$$
**[[3.3 Separation of Variables#^211eff|Condition (i)]] now demands that D equal zero**, so $$V(x, y)=C e^{-k x} sin k y . \tag{3.28}$$ ^8b63a3

Meanwhile, [[3.3 Separation of Variables#^211eff|condition (ii)]] yields $\sin k a=0$ , from which it follows that $$k=\frac{n \pi}{a} \quad(n=1,2,3, ...) . \tag{3.29}$$ ^49e0b8

当我们将边界条件带入的时候，我们就能一步步将积分得到的常数消去。

(At this point you can see **why I chose $C_{1}$ positive and $C_{2}$ negative**:<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;"> if X were sinusoidal, we could never arrange for it to go to zero at infinity</mark>, and <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">if Y were exponential we could not make it vanish at both 0 and a</mark>. Incidentally, $n=0$ is no good, for in that case the potential vanishes everywhere. And we have already excluded negative ns.)

解的设法也很重要，不同的假设会得到不同的结果。

<mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">Laplace’s equation is linear</mark>, in the sense that if $V_{1}, V_{2}, V_{3}, ...$ satisfy it, **so does any linear combination**, $V=\alpha_{1} V_{1}+\alpha_{2} V_{2}+\alpha_{3} V_{3}+\cdots$ , where $\alpha_{1}, \alpha_{2}, ...$ are arbitrary constants. For $$\nabla^{2} V=\alpha_{1} \nabla^{2} V_{1}+\alpha_{2} \nabla^{2} V_{2}+\cdots=0 \alpha_{1}+0 \alpha_{2}+\cdots=0 .$$

Exploiting this fact, we can <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">patch together the separable solutions (Eq. 3.28) to construct a much more general solution</mark>: $$V(x, y)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_{n} e^{-n \pi x / a} sin (n \pi y / a) .\tag{3.30}$$

由于拉普拉斯方程的线性性，任意解的线性组合仍然是原方程的解，因此我们可以将所有不同的 $k$ 代表的解组合起来，形成一个更加普遍的解。

**This still satisfies three of the boundary conditions**; but can we (by astute choice of the coefficients $C_{n}$ ) **fit the final boundary [[3.3 Separation of Variables#^211eff|condition (iii)]]**, $$V(0, y)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_{n} sin (n \pi y / a)=V_{0}(y) ? \tag{3.31}$$
Well, you may recognize this sum – <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">it’s a Fourier sine series</mark>. And Dirichlet’s theorem guarantees that virtually any function $V_{0}(y)$ – it can even have a finite number of discontinuities – can be expanded in such a series. ^056620

显然，上式是某个函数的 [[../../../Mathematic Methods for Physics/notes/05 傅里叶变换/5.1 傅里叶级数|傅里叶级数展开式]]，接下来，我们只需要将傅里叶系数求解出来即可。

used essentially the same idea somewhat earlier. Here’s how it goes: multiply Eq. 3.31 by $sin (n' \pi y / a)$ (where $n'$ is a positive integer), and <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">integrate from 0 to a</mark> $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_{n} \int_{0}^{a} sin (n \pi y / a) sin \left(n' \pi y / a\right) d y=\int_{0}^{a} V_{0}(y) sin \left(n' \pi y / a\right) d y .\tag{3.32}$$
the answer is $$\int_{0}^{a} sin (n \pi y / a) sin \left(n' \pi y / a\right) d y= \begin{cases}0 & \text{if}\ n' \neq n, \\ a / 2 & \text{if}\ n'=n .\end{cases}\tag{3.33}$$

Thus **all the terms in the series drop out, save only the one where $n=n'$** , and the left-hand side of Eq. 3.32, reduces to $(a / 2) C_{n'}$ . *Conclusion* : $$C_{n}=\frac{2}{a} \int_{0}^{a} V_{0}(y) sin (n \pi y / a) d y . \tag{3.34}$$

That does it: <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">Eq. 3.30 is the solution, with coefficients given by Eq. 3.34</mark>. As a **concrete example**, **suppose the strip at $x=0$ is a metal plate with constant potential $V_{0}$** (remember, it’s insulated from the grounded plates at $y=0$ and $y=a$ ). Then $$\begin{aligned} C_{n}=\frac{2 V_{0}}{a} \int_{0}^{a} sin (n \pi y / a) d y & =\frac{2 V_{0}}{n \pi}(1-cos n \pi) \\ & =\left\{\begin{array}{ll} 0 & \text{if n is even}, \\ 4 V_{0} / n \pi & \text{if n is odd}. \end{array} \quad\right. \end{aligned}$$ 
Thus, $$V(x, y)=\frac{4 V_{0}}{\pi} \sum_{n=1,3,5, ...} \frac{1}{n} e^{-n \pi x / a} sin (n \pi y / a) . \tag{3.36}$$

Figure 3.18 is a plot of this potential; Fig. 3.19 shows how the first few terms in the Fourier series combine to make a better and better approximation to the constant $V_{0}$ : (a) is $n=1$ only, (b) includes n up to 5, (c) is the sum of the first 10 terms, and (d) is the sum of the first 100 terms.

## When to use this method ?

The success of this method hinged on two extraordinary properties of the separable solutions ([[3.3 Separation of Variables#^8b63a3|Eqs. 3.28]] and [[3.3 Separation of Variables#^49e0b8|3.29]]): <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">completeness and orthogonality.</mark> 

A set of functions $f_{n}(y)$ is said **to be complete if any other function $f(y)$ can be expressed as a linear combination of them**: $$f(y)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_{n} f_{n}(y) . \tag{3.38}$$

The **functions $sin (n \pi y / a)$ are complete on the interval $0 ≤y ≤a$** . It was this fact, guaranteed by Dirichlet’s theorem, that assured us [[3.3 Separation of Variables#^056620|Eq. 3.31]] could be satisfied, given the proper choice of the coefficients $C_{n}$ . 

**A set of functions is orthogonal if the integral of the product of any two distinct members of the set is zero**: $$\int_{0}^{a} f_{n}(y) f_{n'}(y) d y=0\qquad \text{for}\ n' \neq n . \tag{3.39}$$

> [!example] Example 3.4. 
> Two **infinitely long grounded metal plates**, again at $y=0$ and $y=a$ , are connected at $x= \pm b$ by metal strips maintained **at a constant potential** $V_{0}$ , as shown in Fig. 3.20 (a thin layer of insulation at each corner prevents them from shorting out). Find the potential inside the resulting rectangular pipe.

![[imgs/exp3.4.png]]

*Solution*. Once again, the configuration is independent of z. Our problem is to solve Laplace’s equation $$\frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial x^{2}}+\frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial y^{2}}=0$$ subject to the boundary conditions $$\left.\begin{array}{cl} (i) V=0 & \text{when}\ y=0, \\ (ii) V=0 & \text{when} y=a, \\ (iii) V=V_{0} & \text{when} x=b, \\ (iv) V=V_{0} & \text{when} x=-b . \end{array}\right\} \quad \tag{3.40}$$

The argument runs as before, up to Eq. 3.27: $$V(x, y)=\left(A e^{k x}+B e^{-k x}\right)(C sin k y+D cos k y) .$$
> 假设解为 $V(x, y) = X(x)Y(y)$  $，代入方程得：$$\frac{X''}{X} = -\frac{Y''}{Y} = k^2 \quad (\text{分离常数设为 } k^2)$ $$Y'' + k^2 Y = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad Y(y) = C \sin(ky) + D \cos(ky)$$ $$X'' - k^2 X = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad X(x) = A e^{kx} + B e^{-kx}$$

This time, however, we cannot set $A=0$ ; the region in question does not extend to $x=\infty$ ,SO $e^{k x}$ is perfectly acceptable. On the other hand, the situation is **symmetric** with respect to x , so $V(-x, y)=V(x, y)$ , and it follows that $A=B$ . Using $$e^{k x}+e^{-k x}=2 \cosh k x,$$ and absorbing $2 A$ into c and D , we have $$V(x, y)=\cosh k x(C \sin k y+D \cos k y) .$$

Boundary conditions (i) and (ii) require, as before, that $D=0$ and $k=n \pi / a$ , so $$V(x, y)=C \cosh (n \pi x / a) \sin (n \pi y / a) . \tag{3.41}$$

Because $V(x, y)$ is even in x , it will automatically meet condition (iv) if it fits (iii). It remains, therefore, to construct the general linear combination, $$V(x, y)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_{n} \cosh (n \pi x / a) \sin (n \pi y / a),$$ and pick the coefficients $C_{n}$ in such a way as to satisfy condition (iii): $$V(b, y)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_{n} \cosh (n \pi b / a) \sin (n \pi y / a)=V_{0} .$$
This is the same problem in Fourier analysis that we faced before; I quote the result from Eq. 3.35: $$C_{n} \cosh (n \pi b / a)= \begin{cases}0 & \text{if n is even}, \\ 4 V_{0} / n \pi & \text{if n is odd}. \end{cases}$$

Conclusion: The potential in this case is given by $$V(x, y)=\frac{4 V_{0}}{\pi} \sum_{n=1,3,5, ...} \frac{1}{n} \frac{\cosh (n \pi x / a)}{\cosh (n \pi b / a)} \sin (n \pi y / a) . \tag{3.42}$$

# 3.3.2 Spherical Coordinates

In the examples considered so far, <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">Cartesian coordinates were clearly appropriate, since the boundaries were planes</mark>. For **round objects**, spherical coordinates are more natural. **In the spherical system, Laplace’s equation reads** #def/electrodynamics/potentials $$\frac{1}{r^{2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^{2} \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2} sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(sin \theta \frac{\partial V}{\partial \theta}\right)+\frac{1}{r^{2} sin ^{2} \theta} \frac{\partial^{2} V}{\partial \phi^{2}}=0 . \tag{3.53}$$
I shall assume the problem has **azimuthal symmetry,** so that **V is independent of $\phi$ in that case**, Eq. 3.53 reduces to $$\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^{2} \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\right)+\frac{1}{sin \theta} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(sin \theta \frac{\partial V}{\partial \theta}\right)=0 . \tag{3.54}$$

若某个势场具有 <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">方位角对称性</mark>（ $V$ 与 $\phi$ 无关），将原来的方程（可能是球坐标系下拉普拉斯方程的完整形式）进行化简，得到仅有两个自由度/未知数的方程。

As before, we look for solutions that are products: $$V(r, \theta)=R(r) \Theta(\theta) . \tag{3.55}$$
Putting this into Eq. 3.54, and **dividing by $V$** , $$\frac{1}{R} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d R}{d r}\right)+\frac{1}{\Theta sin \theta} \frac{d}{d \theta}\left(sin \theta \frac{d \Theta}{d \theta}\right)=0 . \tag{3.56}$$
Since **the first term depends only on $r$** , and **the second only on $\theta$** , it follows that **each must be a constant**: $$\frac{1}{R} \frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d R}{d r}\right)=l(l+1), \frac{1}{\Theta sin \theta} \frac{d}{d \theta}\left(sin \theta \frac{d \Theta}{d \theta}\right)=-l(l+1) . \tag{3.57}$$
Here $l(l+1)$ <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">is just a fancy way of writing the separation constant</mark> – you’ll see in a minute why this is convenient.

As always, separation of variables has **converted a partial differential equation (3.54) into ordinary differential equations (3.57)**. The radial equation, $$\frac{d}{d r}\left(r^{2} \frac{d R}{d r}\right)=l(l+1) R, \tag{3.58}$$[[3.3 Separation of Variables#^a6dc05|has the general solution]] $$R(r)=A r^{l}+\frac{B}{r^{l+1}}, \tag{3.59}$$as you can easily check; A and B are the two arbitrary constants to be expected in the solution of a second-order differential equation. But the angular equation, $$\dfrac{d}{d \theta}\left(\sin \theta \dfrac{d \Theta}{d \theta}\right)=-l(l+1) \sin \theta \Theta, \tag{3.60}$$ is not so simple. The solutions are <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Legendre polynomials</mark> #def/electrodynamics/potentials  in the variable $\cos \theta$ : $$\Theta(\theta)=P_{l}(\cos \theta) . \tag{3.61}$$
The function $P_{l}(x)$ is most conveniently defined by the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Rodrigues formula</mark> #def/electrodynamics/potentials : $$P_{l}(x) \equiv \frac{1}{2^{l} l !}\left(\dfrac{d}{d x}\right)^{l}\left(x^{2}-1\right)^{l} \tag{3.62}.$$
The first few Legendre polynomials are listed in **Table 3.1**. Notice that $P_{l}(x)$ is (as the name suggests) an **lth-order polynomial** in x ; it <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">contains only even powers if l is even</mark>, and <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">odd powers if l is odd</mark>. The factor in front, $(1 / 2^{l} l !)$ , was chosen (historically) in order that $$P_{l}(1)=1 . \tag{3.63}$$ ^030802

**Table 3.1**. Legendre polynomials. $$\begin{align}
P_{0}(x)&=1 \\
P_{1}(x)&=x \\
P_{2}(x)&=\left(3 x^{2}-1\right) / 2 \\
P_{3}(x)&=\left(5 x^{3}-3 x\right) / 2 \\
P_{4}(x)&=\left(35 x^{4}-30 x^{2}+3\right) / 8 \\
P_{5}(x)&=\left(63 x^{5}-70 x^{3}+15 x\right) / 8
\end{align}$$ ^29fa00

The Rodrigues formula obviously works **only for nonnegative integer values of $l$**. Moreover, it **provides us with only one solution**. But Eq. 3.60 is second order, and it should possess two independent solutions, for every value of $l$ . It turns out that **these “other solutions” blow up at $\theta=0$ and/or $\theta=\pi$ , and are therefore unacceptable on physical grounds.** For instance, the second solution for $l=0$ is $$\Theta(\theta)=\ln \left(\tan \dfrac{\theta}{2}\right) . \tag{3.64}$$

In the case of azimuthal symmetry, then, the most general separable solution to Laplace’s equation, consistent with minimal physical requirements, is $$V(r, \theta)=\left(A r^{l}+\frac{B}{r^{l+1}}\right) P_{l}(\cos \theta) .$$ (There was no need to include an overall constant in [[3.3 Separation of Variables#^030802|Eq. 3.61]] because it **can be absorbed into A and B** at this stage.) As before, separation of variables yields an infinite set of solutions, one for each $l$ . The **general solution is a linear combination of separable solutions**: $$V(r, \theta)=\sum_{l=0}^{\infty}\left(A_{l} r^{l}+\frac{B_{l}}{r^{l+1}}\right) P_{l}(\cos \theta) \tag{3.65}$$

> [!note] Form Eq. 3.58 to Eq. 3.59
> 
> 给定径向方程：$\dfrac{d}{dr}\left(r^{2} \dfrac{dR}{dr}\right) = l(l+1) R$ 这是一个关于变量 $r$ 的二阶常微分方程，属于**欧拉方程**（Euler equation）类型，其 **特点是自变量 $r$ 以幂次形式出现在系数中**。
> 
> 1. **设解的形式**
> 
> 欧拉方程的通解通常假设为幂函数形式，设：$R(r) = r^k$ 其中 $k$ 为待定常数。
> 
> - 第一步求导：$\dfrac{dR}{dr} = k r^{k-1}$ 
> - 第二步求导（计算左边项）：$\dfrac{d}{dr}\left(r^{2} \dfrac{dR}{dr}\right) = \dfrac{d}{dr}\left(r^{2} \cdot k r^{k-1}\right) = \dfrac{d}{dr}\left(k r^{k+1}\right) = k(k+1) r^{k}$ 
> - 右边项为：$l(l+1) R = l(l+1) r^k$ 
> - 等式两边相等，得到**特征方程**：$k(k+1) = l(l+1)$
> 
> 2. **求解特征方程**
> 
> 特征方程是关于 k 的二次方程：$k^2 + k - l(l+1) = 0$ 利用求根公式或因式分解（观察法），可得两个根：$k_1 = l, \quad k_2 = -(l+1)$ 
> 
> 3. **构造通解**
> 
> 由于方程是线性齐次的，通解为两个独立特解的线性组合：$R(r) = A r^{k_1} + B r^{k_2} = A r^{l} + B r^{-(l+1)}$ 其中 A 和 B 为任意常数（由边界条件确定），这符合二阶微分方程通解包含两个常数的性质。
> 
> 4. **验证解的正确性**
> 
> 将 $R(r) = A r^l + B r^{-(l+1)}$ 代入 Eq.3.58：
> 
> - 对第一项 $A r^l$：$\dfrac{d}{dr}\left(r^2 \cdot A l r^{l-1}\right) = A l \dfrac{d}{dr}\left(r^{l+1}\right) = A l (l+1) r^l = l(l+1) A r^l$
> - 对第二项 $B r^{-(l+1)}$：$\dfrac{d}{dr}\left(r^2 \cdot B (-l-1) r^{-(l+2)}\right) = B (-l-1) \dfrac{d}{dr}\left(r^{-l}\right) = B (-l-1)(-l) r^{-(l+1)} = l(l+1) B r^{-(l+1)}$
> 
> 两项均满足原方程，故解正确。
> 
> 5. **总结**
> 
> 通过**假设幂函数形式的解**，**代入径向方程**得到特征方程，**求解特征根**后**构造线性组合**，最终得到通解：$R(r) = A r^{l} + \dfrac{B}{r^{l+1}} \tag{3.59}$ 这一过程利用了欧拉方程的标准解法，通过分离变量将偏微分方程转化为常微分方程，并通过特征方程求解得到通解。
