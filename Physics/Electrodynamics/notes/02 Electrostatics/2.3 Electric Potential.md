# 2.3.2 Comments on Potential

## 1 The name

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=8,0,12,1&color=example)
> > The word “potential” is a hideous misnomer because it **inevitably reminds you of potential energy**.
> 
> **势** 让你想起 **势能**

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=19,7,21,22&color=example)
> > the best I can do is to insist once and for all that **“potential” and “potential energy” are completely different terms** and should, by all rights, have different names. 
> 
> **势** 和 **势能** 是完全不同的两个物理量

> [!PDF|def] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=21,23,25,1&color=def)
> > incidentally, a region (typically a surface) over which **the potential is constant** is called an **equipotential**.

## 2 Advantage of the potential formulation

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=29,0,48,0&color=example)
> > If you know V , you can easily get E – just take the gradient: E = −∇V 
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=66,16,75,28&color=example)
> >  How can one function possibly contain all the information that three independent functions carry?
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=81,0,92,29&color=note)
> > in fact, **they are explicitly interrelated** by the very condition we started with, **∇ × E = 0**. In terms of the components,
> 
> ![02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&rect=205,392,389,419&color=note)
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=152,1,154,61&color=note)
> >  What the potential formulation does is to **exploit this feature to maximum advantage**, reducing a vector problem to a scalar one, in which there is no need to fuss with components

> > Changing reference points **amounts to adding a constant K** to the potential:

> 
> > [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=242,0,259,0&color=example)
> > where K is the **line integral of E from the old reference point O to the new one O′**

> [!PDF|note] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=259,12,267,19&color=note)
> >  adding a constant to V will **not affect the potential difference between two points**:
> 
> ![02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&rect=227,197,363,216&color=note)
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=304,15,323,1&color=note)
> > Actually, it was already clear from [eq 2.22](2.3%20Electric%20Potential.md#^95f885) that **the potential difference is independent of O**, because it **can be written as the line integral of E from a to b**, with no mention of O.
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=323,2,328,0&color=note)
> >  Nor does the ambiguity affect the gradient of V 
> 
> ![02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&rect=263,117,321,136&color=note)

> [!PDF|important] [02 Electrostatics, p.22](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=22&selection=350,0,353,1&color=important)
> > Potential as such **carries no real physical significance**, for at any given point **we can adjust its value at will by a suitable relocation of O**
> 
> > [!PDF|important] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=9,50,22,21&color=important)
> > The only **quantity with intrinsic meaning** is the **difference in altitude between two points**, and that is the same whatever your reference level.

> [!PDF|note] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=23,27,34,13&color=note)
> > there is a “natural” spot to use for O in electrostatics – analogous to sea level for altitude – and that is **a point infinitely far from the charge**. Ordinarily, then, we “**set the zero of potential at infinity.**”
> 
> 对于静电问题，我们通常选取 **无穷远处为势能零点**

> [!PDF|important] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=49,15,51,38&color=important)
> >  But I must warn you that there is **one special circumstance in which this convention fails**: when **the charge distribution itself extends to infinit**
> 
> 但是，当电荷分布延伸到无穷远处时，通常没办法选取无穷远点作为势能零点

## 4 Potential obeys the superposition principle

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=129,0,140,13&color=example)
> >  The original superposition principle pertains to the force on a test charge Q. It says that the total force on Q is the vector sum of the forces attributable to the source charges individually:
> 
> $$F = F_{1} + F_{2} + \cdots$$
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=159,0,163,24&color=example)
> > Dividing through by Q, we find that the electric field, too, obeys the superposition principle:
> 
> $$E = E_{1} + E_{2} + \cdots$$
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=182,0,186,42&color=example)
> > Integrating from the common reference point to r, it follows that the potential also satisfies such a principle:
> 
> $$V = V_{1} + V_{2} + \cdots$$

## 5 Units of Potential

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=217,0,219,65&color=example)
> >  In our units, force is measured in newtons and charge in coulombs, so electric fields are in newtons per coulomb
> 
> > [!PDF|def] [02 Electrostatics, p.23](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=23&selection=219,66,224,0&color=def)
> >  Accordingly, potential is **newton-meters per coulomb,** or **joules per coulomb**. A joule per coulomb is a **volt**

# Summary

[nodes]

- Electric Potential
	- Definition
		- $V(r) = - \int_{O}^{r}\mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}$ 
		- Set the **infinity** as the **Zero Potential**
	- Relation with E
		- $\mathbf{E} = - \nabla V$ 
	- Comments
		- **Potential** is different from **Potential Energy** 
		- Potential is based on the formula $\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = 0$ 
		- Different reference point gives the different potential, but **their difference remains the same** 
		- Potential obeys the **Superposition Principle** 
		- The unit of Potential is **Volt** 
	- Poisson's Equation and Laplace's Equation
		- Poisson's Equation - $\nabla^{2}V = - \dfrac{\rho}{\epsilon_{0}}$ 
		- Laplace's Equation - $\nabla^{2}V = 0$ 
		- Relation between two equations : The Poisson's Equation is the normal form when **there are charge distributions in the region**, while laplace's equation is the specific form when **there is no charge distribution in the region** .
	- Get Potential from Poisson's Equation - Invert the relationship
		- Normal Form - $V(r) = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \int \dfrac{1}{r}dq$ 
		- Volume - $V(r) = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \int \dfrac{\rho(r)}{r}d\tau$ 
		- Surface - $V(r) = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \int \dfrac{\rho(r)}{r}da$ 
		- Line - $V(r) = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{0}} \int \dfrac{\rho(r)}{r}dl$
	- Boudnary Conditions
		- Electric Field
			- Normal Component
				- **Discontinuous** at the surface
				- The difference between above and bellow is $E^{\perp}_{above} - E^{\perp}_{below} = \dfrac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{0}}$ 
			- Tangential Component
				- **Continuous** at the surface
				- The value above and bellow is equal $E^{\parallel}_{above} = E^{\parallel}_{below}$ 
			- Combination
				- $\mathbf{E_{above} - E_{below}} = \dfrac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{0}}\mathbf{\hat{n}}$ 
		- Electric Potential
			- **Continuous** since $V_{above} - V_{below} = - \int_{a}^{b}\mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0, \Delta l = |b - a| \rightarrow 0$ 
		- The Gradient of Electric Potential
			- **Discontinuous** inherits from electric field since $\nabla V_{above} - \nabla V_{below} = (-\mathbf{E_{above}}) - (-\mathbf{E_{below}}) = -\dfrac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{0}}\mathbf{\hat{n}}$
