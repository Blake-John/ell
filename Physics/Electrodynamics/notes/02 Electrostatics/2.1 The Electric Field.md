# 2.1.1 Introduction

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.1](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=1&selection=7,0,38,2&color=example)
> > The fundamental problem electrodynamics hopes to solve is this (Fig. 2.1): We have some electric charges, q1 , q2 , q3 , . . . (call them **source charges**); what **force do they exert on** another charge, Q (call it the **test charge**)?
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.1](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=1&selection=38,2,48,42&color=example)
> >  The positions of the source charges are given (as functions of time); the **trajectory** of the test particle is **to be calculated**. In general, both the source charges and the test charge are in motion.
> 
> 给定位置即可计算粒子的运动轨迹
> 
> ![02 Electrostatics, p.2](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=2&rect=143,561,317,668&color=example)

> [!PDF|note] [02 Electrostatics, p.1](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=1&selection=49,0,54,37&color=note)
> > The solution to this problem is facilitated by the **principle of superposition**, which states that the **interaction** between any two charges is completely **unaffected by the presence of others**.
> 
> 叠加原理，粒子之间的相互作用不会因为其他粒子的存在而改变

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.1](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=1&selection=54,38,117,36&color=example)
> > This means that **to determine the force on Q**, we can **first compute the force F1** , due to q1 alone (ignoring all the others); **then we compute the force F2** , due to q2 alone; **and so on**. Finally, we **take the vector sum of all these individual forces: F = F1 + F2 + F3 + · · · .** Thus, if we can find the force on Q due to a single source charge q, we are, in principle, done (the rest is just a question of repeating the same operation over and over, and adding it all up)
> 
> 利用叠加原理可以先算分力再算合力

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.1](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=1&selection=119,42,127,23&color=example)
> >  Why don’t I just write down the formula for the force on Q due to q, and be done with it? 
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.1](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=1&selection=132,0,165,41&color=example)
> > **not only** does the force on Q **depend on the separation distance** between the charges (Fig. 2.2), it also depends on both their velocities and on the acceleration of q. Moreover, it is not the position, velocity, and acceleration of q right now that matter: electromagnetic “news” travels at the speed of light, so **what concerns Q is the position, velocity, and acceleration q had at some earlier time, when the message left**.
> 
> 电荷间的作用力并非仅由距离决定，速度和加速度也会对其产生影响，并且由于电磁信号传播速度的限制，电荷 Q 感受到的是电荷 q 发出信号时刻的状态信息。

![02 Electrostatics, p.2](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=2&rect=327,562,425,650&color=example)

> [!PDF|example] [02 Electrostatics, p.2](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/02%20Electrostatics.pdf#page=2&selection=27,52,37,39&color=example)
> > To begin with, we shall consider the special case of **electrostatics** in which **all the source charges are stationary** (though the test charge may be moving).

# Summary

[nodes]

- What is a electric field
	- generated by the source charge
	- some entities fill the space
	- obey the principle of superposition
- Coulomb's Law
	- $\mathbf{F(r)} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{qQ}{r^{2}}\hat{r}$ 
- Electric Field
	- $\mathbf{E(r)} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{q}{r^{2}}\hat{r}$ 
- Continuous Charge Distributions
	- $\mathbf{E(r)} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_{0}}\int \dfrac{1}{r^{2}}\hat{r}dq$ 
	- Line, with linear-charge-density $\lambda$ 
		- $\mathbf{E(r)} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_{0}}\int \dfrac{1}{r^{2}}\hat{r}\lambda dl$ 
	- Surface, with surface-charge-density $\sigma$ 
		- $\mathbf{E(r)} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_{0}}\int \dfrac{1}{r^{2}}\hat{r}\sigma da$ 
	- Volume, with volume-charge-density $\rho$ 
		- $\mathbf{E(r)} = \dfrac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_{0}}\int \dfrac{1}{r^{2}}\hat{r}\rho d\tau$
