# 7.3.1 Electrodynamics before Maxwell

So far, we have encountered the following laws, **specifying the divergence and curl of electric and magnetic fields**: 

$$\begin{align}
&(i) && \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\rho / \varepsilon_{0} && \text{(Gauss's law)}, \\
&(ii) && \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}=0 && \text{(no name)},  \\
&(iii) && \nabla × \mathbf{E}=-\partial \mathbf{B} / \partial t && \text{(Faraday's law)},  \\
&(iv)&& \nabla × \mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{J} && \text{(Ampère's law)}.
\end{align}
$$

- **高斯定律**：$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\dfrac{\rho}{\varepsilon_{0}}$，该定律表明 **电场的散度与电荷密度相关**，即电场线 **从正电荷出发，终止于负电荷**，描述了电荷是如何产生电场的。
- **（磁场的散度定律）**：$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$，意味着 **磁场是无源场**，磁场线是 **闭合曲线**，**没有磁单极子存在**，即不存在单独的磁北极或磁南极。
- **法拉第定律**：$\nabla \times \mathbf{E}=-\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$，说明 **变化的磁场会产生电场**，揭示了电磁感应现象的本质，是发电机等电磁设备的理论基础。
- **安培定律**：$\nabla \times \mathbf{B}=\mu_{0}\mathbf{J}$，建立了磁场的旋度与电流之间的联系，用于计算 **电流产生的磁场分布**。

Now, it happens that<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;"> there is a fatal inconsistency in these formulas</mark>. It has to do with the old rule that divergence of curl is always zero. If you **apply the divergence to number (iii)**, everything works out: $$\nabla \cdot(\nabla \times \mathbf{E})=\nabla \cdot\left(-\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\right)=-\dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}) .$$The **left-hand side is zero because divergence of curl is zero; the right-hand side is zero by virtue of equation (ii)**. But **when you do the same thing to number (iv)**, you get into trouble: $$\nabla \cdot(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})=\mu_{0}(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J})  \tag{7.45}$$the **left-hand side must be zero, but the right-hand side, in general, is not**.  ^cfad48

For **steady currents, the divergence of $\mathbf{J}$ is zero, but when we go beyond magnetostatics Ampère’s law cannot be right**.

There’s another way to see that <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">Ampère’s law is bound to fail for nonsteady currents</mark>. Suppose we’re in the process of charging up a capacitor (Fig. 7.43). In integral form, Ampère’s law reads $$\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=\mu_{0} I_{enc} .$$I want to apply it to the Ampèrian loop shown in the diagram. How do I determine $I_{enc}$ ? 

![[imgs/amperian_loop.png]] ^b4fa78

Well, it’s the total current passing through the loop, or, more precisely, the current piercing a surface that has the loop for its boundary. In this case, **the simplest surface lies in the plane of the loop – the wire punctures this surface, so $I_{enc}=I$** . Fine – but **what if I draw instead the bubble-shaped surface in Fig. 7.43**? ***No* current passes through this surface, and I conclude that $I_{enc}=0$ !** 

<mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">We never had this problem in magnetostatics because the conflict arises only when charge is piling up somewhere (in this case, on the capacitor plates)</mark>. **But for nonsteady currents (such as this one) “the current enclosed by the loop”is an ill-defined notion**; it depends entirely on what surface you use. (If this seems pedantic to you – “obviously one should use the plane surface” – remember that the Ampèrian loop could be some contorted shape that doesn’t even lie in a plane.)

在磁静学中，也就是稳恒电流的情况下，不会出现这种矛盾。因为 **稳恒电流中电荷不会在某一处堆积**，电流在电路中是稳定流动的，对于任何以安培环路为边界的曲面，穿过它们的电流总量都是相同的，不存在因曲面选择不同而导致 $I_{enc}$ 取值不同的问题 。

但在非稳恒电流的情况下，比如给电容器充电时，电荷会在电容器极板上堆积。这就使得 “被回路包围的电流” 这个概念变得模糊不清，其 **数值完全取决于所选择的曲面**。

# 7.3.2 How Maxwell Fixed Ampère’s Law

The problem is on the right-hand side of [[7.3 Maxwell's Equations#^cfad48|Eq. 7.45]] , which should be zero, but isn’t. Applying the **continuity equation** ([[../05 Magnetostatics/5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^4eea25|Eq. 5.29]]) and **Gauss’s law,** the offending term can be rewritten: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}=-\dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}=-\dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\varepsilon_{0} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}\right)=-\nabla \cdot\left(\varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\right) .$$If we were to combine $\varepsilon_{0}(\partial \mathbf{E} / \partial t)$ with $\mathbf{J}$, in Ampère’s law, it would be **just right to kill off the extra divergence**: $$
\nabla \cdot(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) = \mu_{0}(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}) = -\nabla \cdot\left(\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\right) \\
$$So, adding **minus of the term above to Ampère’s Law**, we have the new <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Ampère’s Law</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics  $$\nabla \times \mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{J}+\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \tag{7.46}$$(Maxwell himself had other reasons for wanting to add this quantity to Ampère’s law. To him, the rescue of the continuity equation was a happy dividend rather than a primary motive. But today we recognize this argument as a far more compelling one than Maxwell’s, which was based on a now-discredited model of the ether.) ^bb9c2f

通过上式的修正，在取散度的时候，我们能够使得 **前后两项相互抵消**，最终使得结果为零。

$$\nabla \cdot(\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) = \nabla \cdot(\mu_{0}\mathbf{J}) + \nabla\left( \mu_{0}\varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \right) = -\nabla\left( \mu_{0}\varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \right) + \nabla\left( \mu_{0}\varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \right) = 0$$

Such a modification changes nothing, as far as magnetostatics is concerned: when $\mathbf{E}$ is constant, we still have $\nabla \times\mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{J}$ . In fact, Maxwell’s term is hard to detect in ordinary electromagnetic experiments, where it must compete for attention with $\mathbf{J}$ – that’s why Faraday and the others never discovered it in the laboratory. However, it plays a crucial role in the propagation of electromagnetic waves, as we’ll see in Chapter 9.

> [!note] 
> **a changing electric field induces a magnetic field.**

Maxwell called his extra term the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">displacement current</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics : $$\mathbf{J}_{d} \equiv \varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} . \tag{7.47}$$(It’s a misleading name; $\varepsilon_{0}(\partial \mathbf{E} / \partial t)$ **has nothing to do with current, except that it adds to $\mathbf{J}$ in Ampère’s law**.) Let’s see now how displacement current resolves the paradox of the charging capacitor ([[7.3 Maxwell's Equations#^b4fa78|Fig. 7.43]]). If the capacitor plates are very close together (I didn’t draw them that way, but the calculation is simpler if you assume this), then the **electric field between them is** $$E=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0}} \sigma=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{Q}{A},$$where $Q$ is the **charge on the plate** and $A$ is its **area**. Thus, between the plates, $$\dfrac{\partial E}{\partial t}=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0} A} \dfrac{d Q}{d t}=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0} A} I .$$Now, the “new, improved” Ampère’s law ([[7.3 Maxwell's Equations#^bb9c2f|Eq. 7.46]]) reads, in integral form, $$\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=\mu_{0} I_{enc}+\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \int\left(\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\right) \cdot d \mathbf{a} . \tag{7.48}$$**If we choose the flat surface, then $E=0$ and $I_{enc}=I$** . **If, on the other hand, we use the balloon-shaped surface, then $I_{enc}=0$ ,but $$\int(\partial E / \partial t) \cdot d a = \int \dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}\left( \dfrac{q}{\varepsilon_{0}A} \right)da = \int \dfrac{dq}{dt}\cdot\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0}A} da = I / \varepsilon_{0}$$So we get the same answer for either surface**, though in the first case it comes from the conduction current, and in the second from the displacement current.

修正过后的安培定律不管我们选择哪一种曲面，都能有一个正确，合理的数值。

> [!example] Example 7.15. 
> Imagine two **concentric metal spherical shells** (Fig. 7.44). The inner one (radius $a$ ) carries a charge $Q(t)$ , and the outer one (radius $b$ ) an opposite charge $-Q(t)$ . The space between them is **filled with ohmic material of conductivity** $\sigma$ , so a radial current flows: $$\mathbf{J}=\sigma \mathbf{E}=\sigma \dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{Q}{r^{2}} \hat{r},\quad I=-\dot{Q}=\int \mathbf{J} \cdot d \mathbf{a}=\dfrac{\sigma Q}{\varepsilon_{0}} .$$This configuration is spherically symmetrical, so the magnetic field has to be zero (the only direction it could possibly point is radial, and $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}=0 \Rightarrow \oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d \mathbf{a}=B(4 \pi r^{2})=0$ , so $\mathbf{B}=0$ . What? I thought currents produce magnetic fields! Isn’t that what Biot–Savart and Ampère taught us? How can there be a $\mathbf{J}$ with no accompanying B?

![[imgs/exp7.15.png]]

**Solution**. 

This is not a static configuration: **$Q$ , $\mathbf{E}$ , and $\mathbf{J}$ are all functions of time; Ampère and Biot–Savart do not apply.** The displacement current $$\mathbf{J}_{d}=\varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}= \dfrac{\dot{Q}}{4\pi r^{2}} \hat{r}=-\sigma \dfrac{Q}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0} r^{2}} \hat{r}$$exactly **cancels the conduction current** (in Eq. 7.46), **and the magnetic field (determined by $\nabla \cdot B=0$ , $\nabla ×B=0$ is indeed zero**.

# 7.3.3 Maxwell's Equations

In the last section we put the finishing touches on <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Maxwell’s equations</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics : $$\begin{array}{ll} (i) & \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0}} \rho & (\text{Gauss's law}), \\ (ii) & \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}=0 & (\text{no name}), \\ (iii) & \nabla × \mathbf{E}=-\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} & (\text{Faraday's law}), \\ (iv) & \nabla × \mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{J}+\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} & (\text{Ampère's law with} \\ && \text{Maxwell's correction}). \end{array} \tag{7.49}$$ ^022fa2

Together with the [[../05 Magnetostatics/5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#5.1.2 Magnetic Forces|Lorentz force law]], $$\mathbf{F}=q(\mathbf{E}+\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}), \tag{7.50}$$they summarize the entire theoretical content of classical electrodynamics (save for some special properties of matter, which we encountered in Chapters 4 and 6). Even the [[../05 Magnetostatics/5.1 The Lorentz Force Law#^4eea25|continuity equation]], $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}=-\dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}, \tag{7.51}$$which is the **mathematical expression of conservation of charge**, can be derived from Maxwell’s equations by applying the divergence to number (iv).

I think it is logically preferable to write $$\left.\begin{array}{ll} (i) & \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0}} \rho, & (iii) & \nabla × \mathbf{E}+\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}=0, \\ (ii) & \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}=0, & (iv) & \nabla × \mathbf{B}-\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{J}, \end{array}\right\} \tag{7.52}$$ with the **fields ($\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$) on the left** and **the sources ( $\rho$ and $J$) on the right**. This notation emphasizes that <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">all electromagnetic fields are ultimately attributable to charges and currents</mark>. Maxwell’s equations tell you how charges produce fields; reciprocally, the force law tells you how fields affect charges.

左边是由电荷，电流产生的场，右边则是产生场的电荷，电流源。

# 7.3.4 Magnetic Charge

There is a pleasing symmetry to Maxwell’s equations; it is particularly striking **in free space, where $\rho$ and $\mathbf{J}$ vanish**: $$\left.\begin{array}{l} \nabla \cdot E=0, & \nabla × E=-\dfrac{\partial B}{\partial t}, \\ \nabla \cdot B=0, & \nabla × B=\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial E}{\partial t} . \end{array}\right\}$$

If you **replace $\mathbf{E}$ by $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ by $-\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \mathbf{E}$ , the first pair of equations turns into the second**, and vice versa. 

麦克斯韦方程组在自由空间（无电荷、无电流）中呈现出显著的对称性。

This **symmetry between $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ is spoiled, though, by the charge term in Gauss’s law and the current term in Ampère’s law**. 

但是对于存在电荷以及电流的普遍情况下，这种对称形式并不成立。

You can’t help wondering why the corresponding quantities are “missing” from $\nabla \cdot B=0$ and $\nabla ×E=-\partial B / \partial t$ . What if we had  
$$\left.\begin{array}{ll} (i) & \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0}} \rho_{e}, & (iii) & \nabla × \mathbf{E}=-\mu_{0} \mathbf{J}_{m}-\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}, \\ (ii) & \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \rho_{m}, & (iv) & \nabla × \mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{J}_{e}+\mu_{0} \varepsilon_{0} \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t} . \end{array}\right\} \tag{7.53}$$Then $\rho_{m}$ would represent<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> the density of magnetic “charge,”</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics  and $\rho_{e}$ **the density of electric charge**; $\mathbf{J}_{m}$ would be the **current of magnetic charge**, and $J_{e}$ the **current of electric charge**. <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Both charges would be conserved</mark>: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_{m}=-\dfrac{\partial \rho_{m}}{\partial t} \quad \text{and} \quad \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_{e}=-\dfrac{\partial \rho_{e}}{\partial t} . \tag{7.54}$$The former follows by application of the divergence to (iii), the latter by taking the divergence of (iv).

若引入**磁荷**（磁单极子）和**磁流**的概念，可进一步扩展方程组的对称性。**磁单极子的不存在性**：麦克斯韦方程组中的 $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ 表明磁场是**无源场**，磁场线始终闭合，这与电场（有源场，电场线始于正电荷、终于负电荷）形成鲜明对比。若存在磁荷，则 $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} \neq 0$ ，磁场线可终止于磁荷，破坏现有磁场的无源特性。

# 7.3.5 Maxwell's Equations in Matter

Maxwell’s equations in the form [[7.3 Maxwell's Equations#^022fa2|7.49]] are complete and correct as they stand. However, when you are **working with materials that are subject to electric and magnetic polarization** there is a more convenient way to write them. For **inside polarized matter there will be accumulations of “bound” charge and current**, over which you exert no direct control. It would be nice to reformulate Maxwell’s equations so as to make explicit reference only to the “free” charges and currents.

We have already learned, from the static case, that an **electric polarization $\mathbf{P}$ produces a bound charge density** $$\rho_{b}=-\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P} \tag{7.56}$$([[../04 Electric Fields in Matter/4.2 The Field of a Polarized Object#^a9ca88|Eq. 4.12]]). Likewise, a **magnetic polarization** (or “magnetization”) $\mathbf{M}$ results in a **bound current** $$J_{b}=\nabla × \mathbf{M} \tag{7.57}$$([[Eq. 6.13]] #tobesolved ). There’s just one new feature to consider in the nonstatic case: **any change in the electric polarization involves a flow of (bound) charge**, which must be included in the total current. 

![[imgs/polarized_material.png]]

For suppose we examine a tiny chunk of polarized material (Fig. 7.48). The polarization introduces a charge density $\sigma_{b}=P$ at one end and $-\sigma_{b}$ at the other ([[../04 Electric Fields in Matter/4.2 The Field of a Polarized Object#^a9ca88|Eq. 4.11]]). **If P now increases a bit, the charge on each end increases accordingly, giving a net current** $$d I=\dfrac{\partial \sigma_{b}}{\partial t} d a_{\perp}=\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial t} d a_{\perp} .$$The current density, therefore, is $$\mathbf{J}_{p}=\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{P}}{\partial t} . \tag{7.58}$$This <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">polarization current</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics  has nothing to do with the bound current $\mathbf{J}_{b}$ . The latter is associated with magnetization of the material and involves the spin and orbital motion of electrons; $\mathbf{J}_{p}$ , **by contrast, is the result of the linear motion of charge when the electric polarization changes**. If P points to the right, and is increasing, then each plus charge moves a bit to the right and each minus charge to the left; the cumulative effect is the polarization current $\mathbf{J}_{p}$

We ought to check that **Eq. 7.58 is consistent with the continuity equation**: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_{p}=\nabla \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{P}}{\partial t}=\dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P})=-\dfrac{\partial \rho_{b}}{\partial t} .$$Yes: The continuity equation is satisfied; **in fact, $\mathbf{J}_{p}$ is essential to ensure the conservation of bound charge**. (Incidentally, a changing magnetization does not lead to any analogous accumulation of charge or current. The bound current $\mathbf{J}_{b}=\nabla ×\mathbf{M}$ varis in respons o hanges in $\mathbf{M}$, to be sure,but that's about it.)

In view of all tis the total charge density can be separated into two parts: $$\rho=\rho_{f}+\rho_{b}=\rho_{f}-\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P}, \tag{7.59}$$and the current density into three parts: $$\mathbf{J}=\mathbf{J}_{f}+\mathbf{J}_{b}+\mathbf{J}_{p}=\mathbf{J}_{f}+\nabla × \mathbf{M}+\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{P}}{\partial t} .\tag{7.60}$$

Gauss's law can now be written as $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\dfrac{1}{\varepsilon_{0}}\left(\rho_{f}-\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P}\right),$$ or $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D}=\rho_{f}, \tag{7.61}$$ where, as in the static case, $$\mathbf{D} \equiv \varepsilon_{0} \mathbf{E}+\mathbf{P} . \tag{7.62}$$
