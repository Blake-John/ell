# 7.2.1 Faraday's Law

In 1831, **Michael Faraday** reported on a series of experiments, including three that (with some violence to history) can be characterized as follows:

1. Experiment1 - He **pulled a loop of wire to the right** through a magnetic field (Fig. 7.21a). A current flowed in the loop.
2. Experiment 2 - He **moved the magnet to the left**, holding the loop still (Fig. 7.21b). Again, a current flowed in the loop.
3. Experiment 3-  With both the **loop and the magnet at rest** (Fig. 7.21c), he **changed the strength of the field** (he used an electromagnet, and varied the current in the coil). Once again, current flowed in the loop.

![[imgs/磁生电实验.png]]

The first experiment, of course, is a straightforward case of motional emf; according to the [[7.1 Electromotive Force#7.1.3 Motional Emf|flux rule]], $$\mathcal{E}=-\dfrac{d \Phi}{d t} .$$
For if the **loop moves, it’s a magnetic force that sets up the emf**, but **if the loop is stationary, the force cannot be magnetic** – <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">stationary charges experience no magnetic forces</mark>. In that case, what is responsible? What sort of field exerts a force on charges at rest? Well, <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">electric fields</mark> do, of course, but there doesn’t seem to be any electric field in sight.

> [!note] 
> 法拉第认为：
> **变化的磁场能够感应出电场**

It is this <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">induced electric</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics  field that accounts for the emf in Experiment 2. Indeed, if (as Faraday found empirically) **the emf is again equal to the rate of change of the flux**, $$\mathcal{E}=\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=-\dfrac{d \Phi}{d t}, \tag{7.23}$$
then $\mathbf{E}$ is related to the change in $\mathbf{B}$ by the equation $$\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=-\int \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d \mathbf{a} . \tag{7.24}$$
This is <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Faraday’s law</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics , in integral form. We can **convert it to differential form by applying [[../01 Vector Analysis/1.3 Integral Calculus#1.3.5 The Fundamental Theorem for Curls|Stokes’ theorem]]** (see footnote to Eq. 2.14): $$\nabla × \mathbf{E}=-\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} . \tag{7.25}$$
Note that Faraday’s law **reduces to the old rule** $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=0$ (or, in differential form, $\nabla ×\mathbf{E}=0$ ) **in the static case (constant B**) as, of course, it should.

在静态情况下，磁场 $\mathbf{B}$ 恒定，微分为0，则退回到环路积分为零，这与 **静电场的旋度为零** 相符合。

In Experiment 3, the magnetic field changes for entirely different reasons, but according to Faraday’s law an electric field will again be induced, giving rise to an emf  $-d \Phi / d t$ . Indeed, one can subsume all three cases (and for that matter any combination of them) into a kind of <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">universal flux rule</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics :
**Whenever (and for whatever reason) the magnetic flux through a loop changes, an emf** $$\mathcal{E}=-\dfrac{d \Phi}{d t} \tag{7.26}$$ **will appear in the loop.**

In Faraday’s **first experiment it’s the Lorentz force law at work**; the emf is magnetic. But in **the other two it’s an electric field** (induced by the changing magnetic field) that does the job.

在实验一中的电动势是由于磁场产生的，磁场将拉动回路的力转化成驱动力；而在后两个实验中，则是由感应电场来驱动电荷的移动。

在电磁感应实验中，**感生电场是具有旋度的** ，因此又可以称为 **涡旋电场** #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics 

> [!example] Example 7.6
> A long cylindrical magnet of length $L$ and radius $a$ carries a uniform magnetization $\mathbf{M}$ parallel to its axis. It passes at constant velocity $v$ through a circular wire ring of slightly larger diameter (Fig. 7.22). Graph the emf induced in the ring, as a function of time.

![[imgs/exp7.6.png]]

**Solution**. The magnetic field is the same as that of a long solenoid with surface current $\mathbf{K}_{b}=\mathbf{M} \hat{\phi}$ . So the field inside is $\mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{M}$ , except near the ends, where it starts to spread out. The **flux through the ring is zero when the magnet is far away**; it **builds up to a maximum** of $\mu_{0} \mathbf{M} \pi a^{2}$ as the **leading end passes through**; and it **drops back to zero as the trailing end emerges** (Fig. 7.23a). The emf is (minus) the derivative of $\Phi$ with respect to time, so it consists of two spikes, as shown in Fig. 7.23b.

![[imgs/solu_of_exp7.6.png]]

But how do we determine the direction of the incudced current exactly ?

There’s a handy rule, called <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Lenz’s law</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics , whose sole purpose is to **help you get the directions right of the induced current** :
> [!note] Lenz's Law
> nature abhors a change in flux.

The <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">induced current will flow in such a direction that the flux it produces tends to cancel the change</mark>. (As the front end of the magnet in Ex. 7.6 enters the ring, the flux increases, so the current in the ring must generate a field to the right – it therefore flows clockwise.) <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Notice that it is the change in flux, not the flux itself, that nature abhors</mark> (when the tail end of the magnet exits the ring, the flux drops, so the induced current flows counterclockwise, in an effort to restore it). 

Faraday induction is **a kind of “inertial” phenomenon**: A **conducting loop “likes” to maintain a constant flux through it**; if you try to change the flux, the loop responds by **sending a current around in such a direction as to frustrate your efforts**. (It doesn’t succeed completely; the flux produced by the induced current is typically only a tiny fraction of the original. All Lenz’s law tells you is the direction of the flow.)

对于楞次定律而言，**“厌恶”的是磁场的变化，而非磁场本身** ，这就像是一种 **惯性** ，感应电流要保持磁通量的大小趋于不变，依次来抵消变化效果，但是又不能完全抵消。

> [!example] Example 7.7
> (The “jumping ring” demonstration.) If you wind a solenoidal coil around an iron core (the iron is there to beef up the magnetic field), **place a metal ring on top, and plug it in, the ring will jump several feet in the air** (Fig. 7.24). Why?

![[imgs/exp7.7.png|300]]

**Solution**. Before you turned on the current, the flux through the ring was zero. Afterward a flux appeared (upward, in the diagram), and the emf generated in the ring led to a current (in the ring) which, according to Lenz’s law, was in such a direction that its field tended to cancel this new flux. This means that the current in the loop is opposite to the current in the solenoid. And opposite currents repel, so the ring flies off.

# 7.2.2 The Induced Electric Field

Faraday’s law generalizes the electrostatic rule $\nabla ×\mathbf{E}=0$ to the time-dependent régime. The **divergence** of $\mathbf{E}$ is still **given by [[../02 Electrostatics/2.2 Divergence and Curl of Electrostatic Fields#Gauss's Law|Gauss’s law]]** $(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\rho / \varepsilon_{0})$ .If $\mathbf{E}$ is a **pure Faraday field** (due exclusively to a changing $\mathbf{B}$, with $\rho=0$ ), then $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=0, \quad \nabla × \mathbf{E}=-\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} .$$
This is mathematically **ientical to magnetostatics**, $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B}=0, \quad \nabla × \mathbf{B}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{J} .$$
**Conclusion**: Faraday-induced electric fields are determined by $-(\partial B / \partial t)$ in exactly **the same way as magnetostatic fields** are determined by $\mu_{0} \mathbf{J}$ . The **analog to [[../05 Magnetostatics/5.2 The Biot-Savart Law|Biot–Savart]]** is $$\mathbf{E}=-\dfrac{1}{4 \pi} \int \dfrac{(\partial \mathbf{B} / \partial t) × \hat{r}}{r^{2}} d \tau=-\dfrac{1}{4 \pi} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial t} \int \dfrac{\mathbf{B} × \hat{r}}{r^{2}} d \tau, \tag{7.27}$$
and **if symmetry permits, we can use all the tricks associated with [[|Ampère’s law]] in integral form** $(\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=\mu_{0} \mathbf{I}_{enc})$ , only now it’s Faraday’s law in integral form: $$\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=-\dfrac{d \Phi}{d t} . \tag{7.28}$$ #tobesolved 

在计算感应电场时，常利用磁静学的方法（如安培定律、毕奥 - 萨伐尔定律等）来计算变化磁场，严格来说这种方法得到的结果只是近似正确的。但在实际应用中，除非磁场波动极快，或者关注的点距离源很远，否则误差通常可以忽略不计。这种可以使用磁静学规则计算法拉第定律右侧磁场的情况被称为磁准静态。然而，当涉及到电磁波和辐射等问题时，就必须认真考虑磁静学方法的失效情况 。

# 7.2.3 Inductance

Suppose you have two loops of wire, at rest (Fig. 7.30). If you **run a steady current $I_{1}$ around loop 1**, it produces a magnetic field $B_{1}$ . **Some of the field lines pass through loop 2**; let $\Phi_{2}$ be the flux of $B_{1}$ through 2. You might have a tough time actually calculating $B_{1}$ , but a glance at the Biot–Savart law, $$B_{1}=\dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} I_{1} \oint \dfrac{d \mathbf{I}_{1} × \hat{r}}{r^{2}},$$
reveals one significant fact about this field: **it is proportional to the current** $I_{1}$ . Therefore, so too is the flux through loop 2: $$\Phi_{2}=\int \mathbf{B}_{1} \cdot d \mathbf{a}_{2} .$$
Thus  
$$\Phi_{2}=M_{21} I_{1}, \quad(7.31)$$
where $M_{21}$ is the **constant of proportionality**; it is known as the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">mutual inductance</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics  of the two loops.

![[imgs/two_loop.png]]

There is a cute formula for the mutual inductance, which you can **derive by expressing the flux in terms of the vector potential**, and **invoking [[../01 Vector Analysis/1.3 Integral Calculus#1.3.5 The Fundamental Theorem for Curls|Stokes’ theorem]]**: $$\Phi_{2}=\int \mathbf{B}_{1} \cdot d \mathbf{a}_{2}=\int\left(\nabla × \mathbf{A}_{1}\right) \cdot d \mathbf{a}_{2}=\oint \mathbf{A}_{1} \cdot d \mathbf{l}_{2} .$$
Now, according to [[Eq. 5.66]] #tobesolved , $$\mathbf{A}_{1}=\dfrac{\mu_{0} I_{1}}{4 \pi} \oint \dfrac{d \mathbf{I}_{1}}{r},$$
hence   
$$\Phi_{2}=\dfrac{\mu_{0} I_{1}}{4 \pi} \oint\left(\oint \dfrac{d \mathbf{l}_{1}}{r}\right) \cdot d \mathbf{l}_{2} .$$
Evidently   
$$M_{21}=\dfrac{\mu_{0}}{4 \pi} \oint \oint \dfrac{d \mathbf{l}_{1} \cdot d \mathbf{l}_{2}}{r} . \tag{7.32}$$
This is the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Neumann formula</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics ; it involves a double line integral – **one integration around loop 1, the other around loop 2** (Fig. 7.31). It’s not very useful for practical calculations, but it does reveal two important things about mutual inductance:
1. $M_{21}$ is a **purely geometrical quantity**, having to do with the sizes, shapes, and relative positions of the two loops.
2. The **integral in Eq. 7.32 is unchanged if we switch the roles of loops 1 and 2**; it follows that $$M_{21}=M_{12} . \tag{7.33}$$
> [!note] 
> This is an astonishing **conclusion**: 
> Whatever the shapes and positions of the loops, the **flux through 2** when we run a curent $I$ around 1 **is identical to the fux through 1** when we send the same current I around 2.
> We may as well drop the subscripts and call them both $M$ .
