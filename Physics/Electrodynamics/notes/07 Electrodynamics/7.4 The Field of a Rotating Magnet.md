Imagine an **axially symmetric magnet rotating at constant angular velocity about its axis** – a cylindrical bar magnet, if you like, or for that matter **the Earth itself (pretending that the magnetic axis coincides with the rotation axis)**. It is natural to wonder **whether its magnetic field is stationary, or rotates with the magnet** (or, as Arlo Guthrie would say, “Maybe it doesn’t even matter”). Are the field lines “dragged along” by the rotation of the source? 

地球是个巨大的磁体，地球沿着转轴自转，我们对于转动中的磁体会不会导致磁场发生变化

Faraday did a series of experiments (variations on the disk experiment of [[7.1 Electromotive Force#^04f99c|Ex. 7.4]]) that appeared to settle the issue. **Mounting the magnet along the axis of the disk he first held the magnet still while he rotated the disk** (that’s the original version), and **a current was generated in the circuit** (Fig. 7.51). 

![[imgs/rotating_magnet.png]]

Then he **allowed the magnet to rotate at the same rate as the disk**; again **a current was generated**. Finally, he **held the disk at rest and rotated the magnet**. This time **no current flowed**. <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">He concluded that the field of the rotating magnet is the same as that of the stationary magnet</mark>: that’s why the first two experiments give the same result, and the third is just what you’d get if the magnet were at rest. 

> [!note] 
> Apparently **a magnet rotating about its axis of symmetry does not drag the field around** as it spins;

法拉第做了3个实验：

1. 转动转盘，固定磁铁 - 转盘中 **出现电流**
2. 同时转动转盘和磁铁 - 转盘中 **出现电流**
3. 固定转盘，转动磁铁 - 转盘中 **不出现电流**

因此他得到了 **沿着对称轴旋转** 的磁体的磁场不会随之旋转的结论。

And yet, if the magnet **rotates about an axis perpendicular to its axis of symmetry**, then it **certainly does drag the field along with it**. The field lines loop around from the north pole to the south pole, and that direction keeps changing. 

但是，如果不沿着对称轴旋转，磁场显然会随着磁铁的旋转而旋转。

So not everyone accepted Faraday’s conclusion, until 1912 when experiments by **S. J. Barnett** finally led to a consensus that Faraday was right – **in particular, the Earth’s magnetic field (or more precisely the part of it associated with rotation about the geographic north-south axis) does not rotate with the Earth**.

But what exactly does it mean to say that the field lines do (or do not) rotate with their source? Faraday had a very concrete conception of field lines as a network of “wires” (or cobwebs) existing “out there” in space. In that case, to say that they are moving (or not) makes perfect sense. But in Maxwell’s theory it is fields that have real physical existence, and field lines are just constructs we devise (by joining up the arrows that represent the fields themselves) to help us visualize (and draw) the fields. And we have already learned that even static magnetic field lines can be problematic ([[|Section 5.3.4]] #tobesolved ).

![[imgs/rotating_e_field.png]]

Consider for a moment the electrical analog. Imagine a point charge at rest at the origin. Its **(electric) field “radiates” out** in all directions, and it is (obviously) constant in time (Fig. 7.52). The field lines are like spokes on a wheel (or rather pins in a pin cushion, in three dimensions). A moment later the picture looks exactly the same. But suppose I ask which field line at $t=1$ corresponds to the field line I labeled a at $t=0$ ? It's $a'$ , obviously (I hear you say). Not so fast! Why not $a^{\prime \prime}$ ? That would still be a perfectly good field-line pattern, representing exactly the same field. The labeling of the field lines is completely arbitrary, with no physical content at all. If you prefer to say that the field line called a (at $t=0$ ) “corresponds” to the field line $a^{\prime \prime}$ at $t=1$ , and then to $a^{\prime \prime \prime}$ at $t=2$ , and so on, more power to you! For you, the field lines rotate, even though the source does not. It’s not wrong, exactly (it describes the field correctly at every moment), it’s just dumb – a complicated and confusing way to think about something that is intrinsically simple and straightforward.

一个点电荷的旋转并不会导致该处的电场强度发生变化，任意时刻的场强仍然保持不变，仍然是一个静电场。

But suppose instead of a point charge we have a uniformly charged sphere, and it’s (uniformly) rotating. The electric field is exactly the same (for points outside), and it’s still static (because the charge density is constant in time). It is now accompanied by a (static) magnetic field, but that doesn’t alter the electric field. Now, however, it is not quite so dumb to wonder whether the electric field lines rotate with the sphere. Well, let ’em rotate, if it pleases you (you can even have them rotate at some completely different rate, about some other axis, if you want to be really perverse); this has nothing to do with physics – they represent the same electric field – it just has to do with how you choose to direct the field-line movie. Arlo was right: it doesn’t even matter. **Field lines tell you two things: their density tells you the magnitude of the field, and the arrow tells you its direction**. As long as they get those two things right, you can’t ask for more, because that’s all there is to know about the fields. Which field line at one moment corresponds to which field line at some other moment … that’s a metaphysical question; it has no “right” answer, because it is devoid of physical content. And for electric fields that’s pretty much the end of the story.

电场线是静态的，只能告诉你在某个地方的场强以及场强的方向，不能告诉你这个地方的场强是怎么来的。

But the magnetic case is more pernicious, because of the $\mathbf{v}$ in **the Lorentz force law**. That’s the velocity of the charge … but relative to what? Nowadays we would answer “relative to whatever inertial reference frame you choose to work in – the frame in which the magnetic field is $\mathbf{B}$.” But if you conceive of magnetic field lines as “wires” out there in space, moving around in time, it is easy to persuade yourself (at any rate, it was in the nineteenth century) that $\mathbf{v}$ must **be the velocity of the charge relative to the field line at that location**. (Remember those electrical engineers talking about “cutting” field lines.) And if that is the case (which, I hasten to say, it is not) then it matters critically whether the field lines are dragged along with the rotation of the magnet. If they are dragged along, then Faraday should have found the same current when the magnet rotated and the disk was at rest as he did when the disk rotated and the magnet was at rest, and he should have seen no. current when the two rotated together. But that’s the opposite of what he actually observed, so he concluded that the field lines are not dragged along by the rotation of the magnet. And similarly Barnett concluded that the Earth does not drag its magnetic field around as it rotates. 

It’s not an unreasonable inference, and in practice it is sometimes an illuminating way to picture how the field evolves in time. That’s why geophysicists, plasma physicists, and astrophysicists use this language. But it is a choice, not something forced by the laws of electrodynamics, so when you read about field lines being “dragged” around, ask yourself what precisely the author means by the statement, and remind yourself that field lines don’t come in different colors (or carry serial numbers) so you could keep track of individual lines from frame to frame in the movie.
