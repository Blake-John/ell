# 7.1.1 Ohm's Law

To make a current flow, you have to push on the charges. How fast they move, in response to a given push, depends on the nature of the material. For most substances, the current density $\mathbf{J}$ is proportional to the **force per unit charge**, $\mathbf{f}$: $$\mathbf{J}=\sigma \mathbf{f} . \tag{7.1}$$
The proportionality factor $σ$ (not to be confused with surface charge) is an **empirical constant that varies from one material to another**; it’s called the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">conductivity</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics  of the medium. Actually, the handbooks usually list the **reciprocal of $σ$** , called the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">resistivity</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics : $\rho=\dfrac{1}{\sigma}$ (not to be confused with charge density – I’m sorry, but we’re running out of Greek letters, and this is the standard notation). Some typical values are listed in Table 7.1.  ^27f2a2

Notice that <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">even insulators conduct slightly</mark>, though the conductivity of a metal is astronomically greater; in fact, for most purposes metals can be regarded as **perfect conductors**, with $\sigma=\infty$ , while for **insulators** we can pretend $\sigma=0$ 

导体和非导体只是相对的，即使是非导体也能轻微的导电，因此我们选择一个物理量——电导率，用于描述物体导电能力的大小。

In principle,<mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;"> the force that drives the charges to produce the current could be anything</mark> – **chemical, gravitational, or trained ants with tiny harnesses**. For our purposes, though, it’s usually an **electromagnetic force** that does the job. In this case Eq. 7.1 becomes $$\mathbf{J}=\sigma(\mathbf{E}+\mathbf{v} × \mathbf{B}) . \tag{7.2}$$

Ordinarily, the **velocity of the charges is sufficiently small** that the second term can be ignored: $$\mathbf{J}=\sigma \mathbf{E} . \tag{7.3}$$
(However, in plasmas, for instance, the magnetic contribution to $\mathbf{f}$ can be significant.) Equation 7.3 is called <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Ohm’s law</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics , though the physics behind it is really contained in Eq. 7.1, of which 7.3 is just a **special case**. ^349803

I know: **you’re confused because I said $E=0$ inside a conductor ([[Section 2.5.1]]). But that’s for stationary charges ( $\mathbf{J}=0$ )**. Moreover, for **perfect conductors $\mathbf{E}=\mathbf{J} / \sigma=0$ even if current is flowing**. 

In practice, **metals are such good conductors that the electric field required to drive current in them is negligible**. Thus we routinely <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">treat the connecting wires in electric circuits (for example) as equipotentials</mark>. **Resistors**, by contrast, are **made from poorly conducting materials**.

对于一个导体而言，只要其具有电阻，就会对电荷（对于具体例子而言是电子）具有束缚作用，那么想要有电流通过，就 **必须要有一个外力来驱动电荷的移动**，这个外力与材料对电荷的束缚力作用效果相反。对于超导体而言，即使没有这个驱动力（外加的电场为0），也能有电流移动，这是因为 $\sigma = \infty, \rho = 0$ 。

As these examples illustrate, the **total current flowing from one electrode to the other is proportional to the potential difference between them**: $$V=I R .\tag{7.4}$$
This, of course, is the more familiar version of Ohm’s law. The constant of proportionality **R** is called the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">resistance</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics ; it’s <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">a function of the geometry of the arrangement and the conductivity of the medium between the electrodes</mark>. (In Ex. 7.1, $R=(L / \sigma A)$ ; in Ex. 7.2, $R=\ln (b / a) / 2 \pi \sigma L .$ ) Resistance is **measured in ohms** $(\Omega)$ ): **an ohm is a volt per ampere**. Notice that the proportionality between $V$ and $I$ is a direct consequence of [[7.1 Electromotive Force#^349803|Eq. 7.3]]: if you want to double V , you simply double the charge on the electrodes – that doubles $\mathbf{E}$ , which (for an ohmic material) doubles $\mathbf{J}$ , which doubles $\mathbf{I}$ 

电阻可以通过电阻率来计算 : $$R = \dfrac{l}{\sigma A} = \dfrac{\rho l}{A}$$

For **steady currents and uniform conductivity**, $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}=\dfrac{1}{\sigma} \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}=0 \tag{7.5}$$
([[Eq. 5.33]]), and therefore the **charge density is zero**; any **unbalanced charge resides on the surface**. (We proved this long ago, for the case of stationary charges, using the fact that $\mathbf{E}=0$ ; turns out, it is still true when steady currents flow, for media satisfying [[7.1 Electromotive Force#^349803|Eq. 7.3]].) It follows, in particular, that <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">Laplace’s equation holds within a homogeneous ohmic material carrying a steady current</mark>, so all the tools and tricks of Chapter 3 are available for calculating the potential.

因为在稳恒电流情况下，电流的 **连续性方程为** $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0$ （该式表示电流在空间中 **没有源或汇**，电流是连续的 ），所以 $\nabla \cdot E=\dfrac{1}{\sigma} \nabla \cdot J = 0$  。由 **高斯定律（Gauss's law）** $\nabla \cdot E=\dfrac{\rho}{\varepsilon_{0}}$ ，已知 $\nabla \cdot E = 0$ ，那么可得 $\rho = 0$ ，即 **电荷密度为零**。这意味着在 **均匀导电介质内部不存在净电荷，任何未平衡的电荷（unbalanced charge）都只能存在于介质的表面**。

# 7.1.2 Electromotive Force

#tobesolved 

There are really two forces involved in driving charge around a circuit: **the source**, $q \mathbf{f}_{s}$ , which is ordinarily **confined to one portion of the loop** (a battery, say), and an **electrostatic force**, $q \mathbf{E}$ , which **serves to smooth out the flow and communicate the influence of the source to distant parts of the circuit**: $$\mathbf{f}=\mathbf{f}_{s}+\mathbf{E} . \tag{7.8}$$
The physical agency responsible for $\mathbf{f}_{s}$ can be many different things: in a **battery it’s a chemical force**; in a piezoelectric crystal**, mechanical pressure is converted into an electrical impulse**; in a thermocouple it’s a **temperature gradient that does the job**; in a photoelectric **cell it’s light**; and in a Van de Graaff generator the electrons are literally loaded onto a conveyer belt and swept along. Whatever the mechanism, its **net effect is determined by the line integral of $\mathbf{f}$ around the circuit**: $$\mathcal{E} \equiv \oint \mathbf{f} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=\oint \mathbf{f}_{s} \cdot d \mathbf{l} .\tag{7.9}$$
(Because $\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=0$ for electrostatic fields, it doesn’t matter whether you use $\mathbf{f}$ or $\mathbf{f}_{s}.$ ) The quantity ε is called the <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">electromotive force</mark> #def/electrodynamics/electrodynamics , or **emf**, of the circuit. It’s a lousy term, since this is <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">not a force at all – it’s the integral of a force per unit charge</mark>. Some people prefer the word **electromotance**, but emf is so established that I think we’d better stick with it.

驱动电荷在电路中流动的力包含两个部分：一是 **局限于回路某一部分的源力** $qf_s$ ，像电池中的化学力、压电晶体中的机械压力转化的电力、热电偶中的温度梯度产生的力、光电管中的光生作用力以及范德格拉夫发生器中电子传送带产生的力等；二是 **用于平衡电流和传递源力影响的静电力** $q\mathbf{E}$ 。

由于静电场中$\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d \mathbf{l} = 0$ （静电场是无旋的，沿回路积分结果为零），所以在计算电动势时，使用 $\mathbf{f}$ 或 $\mathbf{f}_s$ 结果相同。需要注意的是，**源力 $\mathbf{f}_{s}$ 并不是真正的力，而是单位电荷受到的力的大小**， **电动势 $\mathcal{E}$ 也并非真正的力，而是单位电荷所受作用力的积分** 。

Within an **ideal source of emf** (a **resistanceless battery**, for instance), the **net force on the charges is zero** ([[7.1 Electromotive Force#^27f2a2|Eq. 7.1]] with $\sigma=\infty$ ) - [[7.1 Electromotive Force#^794b9a|see also]] , so $\mathbf{E}=-\mathbf{f}_{s}$ . The **potential difference between the terminals** ( $a$ and $b$ ) is therefore $$V=-\int_{a}^{b} \mathbf{E} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=\int_{a}^{b} \mathbf{f}_{s} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=\oint \mathbf{f}_{s} \cdot d \mathbf{l}=\mathcal{E} \tag{7.10}$$
(we can extend the integral to the entire loop **because $\mathbf{f}_{s}=0$ outside the source**). The <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">function of a battery, then, is to establish and maintain a voltage difference equal to the electromotive force</mark> (a 6 V battery, for example, holds the positive terminal 6 V above the negative terminal). The <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">resulting electrostatic field drives current around the rest of the circuit</mark> (notice, however, that **inside the battery $\mathbf{f}_{s}$ drives current in the direction opposite to $\mathbf{E}$**).

电池的作用是 **建立并维持与电动势相等的电压差**，进而 **驱动电流在电路其余部分流动** 。不过在电池内部， $\mathbf{f}_s$ 驱动电流的方向与 $\mathbf{E}$相反。

> [!note] 为什么电池内部电荷受力为零
> 在理想电动势源（如无内阻电池）中，根据公式 $\mathbf{J} = \sigma \mathbf{f}$ ，当电导率$\sigma = \infty$时，意味着电流可以在无阻碍的情况下流动。因为电流密度 $\mathbf{J}$ 是 **有限值** （实际电路中电流不会无限大），要使等式成立，此时 **单位电荷所受的合力 $\mathbf{f}$ 必须为零**。而驱动电荷的力包含电源力 $\mathbf{f}_s$ 和静电力 $\mathbf{E}$ ，即 $\mathbf{f} = \mathbf{f}_s + \mathbf{E}$，$\mathbf{f} = 0$就导致$\mathbf{E} = -\mathbf{f}_s$ 。这表明在理想电动势源内部，**静电力和电源力大小相等、方向相反** 。
^794b9a

However, as you’ll see in the next section, there is some subtlety involved in this interpretation, so I prefer Eq. 7.9.

# 7.1.3 Motional Emf

**Generators** exploit **motional emfs**, which arise when you move a wire through a magnetic field. Figure 7.10 suggests a primitive model for a generator. In the shaded region there is a **uniform magnetic field** $\mathbf{B}$, **pointing into the page**, and the resistor R represents whatever it is (maybe a light bulb or a toaster) we’re trying to drive current through. If the entire loop is **pulled to the right with speed** $\mathbf{v}$ , the mobile charges in segment $a b$ experience a magnetic force whose vertical component $qvB$ drives current around the loop, in the clockwise direction. The emf is $$\mathcal{E}=\oint \mathbf{f}_{mag} \cdot d l=v B h, \tag{7.11}$$
where h is the width of the loop. (The horizontal segments $b c$ and ad contribute nothing, since the force there is perpendicular to the wire.)
