# 4.2.1 Bound Charges

Suppose we have a piece of polarized material – *Question* : What is the field produced by this object (not the field that may have *caused* the polarization, but the field the polarization *itself* causes)?

Well, we know what the field of an individual dipole looks like, so why not **chop the material up into infinitesimal dipoles and integrate to get the total**? As usual, it’s **easier to work with the potential**. For a single dipole $\mathbf{p}$ ([[Eq. 3.99]]), $$V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{\mathbf{p} \cdot \hat{r}}{r^{2}}, \tag{4.8}$$where $\hat{r}$ is the vector **from the dipole to the point at which we are evaluating the potential** (Fig. 4.8). In the present context, we have a dipole moment $\mathbf{p}= \mathbf{P} d \tau'$ in each volume element $d \tau'$ , so the total potential is  
$$V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \int_{\mathcal{V}} \dfrac{\mathbf{P}\left(r'\right) \cdot \hat{r}}{r^{2}} d \tau' .\tag{4.9}$$ ^54b9c2

![[imgs/dipole_with_d.png]]

我们仍然通过考虑电势变化来获得电场，由于在一个体积元中的磁矩为 $\mathbf{P}d\tau'$ ，通过积分即可得到总的电势分布。

That does it, in principle. But a little sleight of hand **casts this integral into a much more illuminating form**. Observing that $$\nabla'\left(\dfrac{1}{r}\right)=\dfrac{\hat{r}}{r^{2}},$$where (unlike Prob. 1.13) the differentiation is with respect to the source coordinates ( $r'$ ), we have $$V=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \int_{\mathcal{V}} \mathbf{P} \cdot \nabla'\left(\dfrac{1}{r}\right) d \tau' .$$
Integrating by parts, using [[../01 Vector Analysis/1.2 Differential Calculus#1.2.6 Product Rules|product rule number 3]] (inside the front cover), gives $$V=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}}\left[\int_{\mathcal{V}} \nabla' \cdot\left(\dfrac{\mathbf{P}}{r}\right) d \tau'-\int_{\mathcal{V}} \dfrac{1}{r}\left(\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{P}\right) d \tau'\right],$$ or, invoking the [[../01 Vector Analysis/1.3 Integral Calculus#1.3.4 The Fundamental Theorem for Divergence|divergence theorem]] , $$V=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \oint_{\mathcal{S}} \dfrac{1}{r} \mathbf{P} \cdot d \mathbf{a}'-\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \int_{\mathcal{V}} \dfrac{1}{r}\left(\nabla' \cdot \mathbf{P}\right) d \tau' . \tag{4.10}$$

由于空间中电势的计算公式为 :

$$V = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0}}\oint_{\mathcal{S}} \dfrac{1}{r}\sigma da = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0}} \oint_{\mathcal{V}} \dfrac{1}{r}\rho d\tau$$

The **first term** looks like the **potential of a surface charge**, $$\sigma_{b} \equiv \mathbf{P} \cdot \hat{n} \tag{4.11}$$ (where $\hat{n}$ is the normal unit vector), while the **second term looks like the potential of a volume charge**, $$\rho_{b} \equiv-\nabla \cdot \mathbf{P} .\tag{4.12}$$

With these definitions, Eq. 4.10 becomes $$V(r)=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \oint_{S} \dfrac{\sigma_{b}}{r} d a'+\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \int_{\mathcal{V}} \dfrac{\rho_{b}}{r} d \tau' .\tag{4.13}$$
This means that the **potential (and hence also the field) of a polarized object** is **the same as that produced by a volume charge density** $\rho_{b}=$ -V·P **plus a surface charge density** $\sigma_{b}=P \cdot \hat{n}$ . 

Instead of integrating the contributions of all the infinitesimal dipoles, as in [[4.2 The Field of a Polarized Object#^54b9c2|Eq. 4.9]] , we could **first find those bound charges**, and then **calculate the fields they produce**, in the same way we calculate the field of any other volume and surface charges (for example, using Gauss’s law).

> [!example] Example 4.2. 
> Find the electric field produced by a uniformly polarized **sphere** of radius $R$.
> 
> *Solution*. 
> We might as well choose the z-axis to coincide with the direction of polarization (Fig. 4.9). The **volume bound charge density $\rho_{b}$ is zero**, since $\mathbf{P}$ is uniform, but $$\sigma_{b}=\mathbf{P} \cdot \hat{n}=P cos \theta,$$ where $\theta$ is the usual spherical coordinate. What we want, then, is the field produced by a charge density $P cos \theta$ plastered over the surface of a sphere. But we already computed the potential of such a configuration ([[Eqs. 3.86]] and [[3.87]]): $$V(r, \theta)= \begin{cases}\dfrac{P}{3 \varepsilon_{0}} r cos \theta & \text{for } r \leq R, \\ \dfrac{P}{3 \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{R^{3}}{r^{2}} cos \theta & \text{for } r \geq R .\end{cases}$$
> 
> Since $r cos \theta=z$ , the field **inside the sphere is uniform**: $$E=-\nabla V=-\dfrac{P}{3 \varepsilon_{0}} \hat{z}=-\dfrac{1}{3 \varepsilon_{0}} \mathbf{P} \qquad \text{for } r<R . \tag{4.14}$$
> 
> This remarkable result will be very useful in what follows. **Outside the sphere the potential is identical to that of a perfect dipole at the origin**, $$V=\dfrac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_{0}} \dfrac{\mathbf{p} \cdot \hat{r}}{r^{2}} \text{for } r \geq R, \tag{4.15}$$ whose dipole moment is, not surprisingly, equal to the **total dipole moment** of the sphere: $$\mathbf{p}=\dfrac{4}{3} \pi R^{3} \mathbf{P} . \tag{4.16}$$
>

# 4.2.2 Physical Interpretation of Bound Charges

In the last section we found that <mark style="background: #ADCCFFA6;">the field of a polarized object is identical to the field that would be produced by a certain distribution of “bound charges."</mark> $\sigma_{b}$ and $\rho_{b}$ . But this conclusion emerged in the course of abstract manipulations of the integral in [[4.2 The Field of a Polarized Object#^54b9c2|Eq. 4.9]], and left us with no clue as to the physical meaning of these bound charges. Indeed, some authors give you the impression that bound charges are in some sense “fictitious” – mere bookkeeping devices used to facilitate the calculation of fields. Nothing could be further from the truth: **$\rho_{b}$ and $\sigma_{b}$ represent perfectly genuine accumulations of charge**. In this section I’ll explain how polarization leads to these charge distributions.
