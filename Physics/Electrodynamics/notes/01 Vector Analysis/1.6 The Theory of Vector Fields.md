# 1.6.1 The Helmholtz Theorem

> [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=6,0,37,32&color=example)
> > Ever since Faraday, the laws of electricity and magnetism have been **expressed in terms of electric and magnetic fields, E and B.** Like many physical laws, these are most compactly **expressed as differential equations**. Since E and B are vectors, **the differential equations naturally involve vector derivatives: divergence and curl**

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=37,33,45,0&color=note)
> >  Indeed, Maxwell **reduced the entire theory to four equations**, specifying respectively the **divergence** and the **curl** of E and B

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=46,0,47,71&color=note)
> > Maxwell’s formulation raises an important mathematical question: To what extent is a vector function **determined by its divergence and curl**?
> 
> 向量函数如何取决于他的散度以及旋度

> [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=48,12,67,1&color=example)
> >  if I tell you that the divergence of F (which stands for E or B, as the case may be) is a specified (scalar) function D,
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&rect=258,467,314,486&color=example)
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=79,0,86,0&color=example)
> > and the curl of F is a specified (vector) function C
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&rect=261,428,311,448&color=example)
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=98,1,102,22&color=example)
> > for consistency, C must be divergenceless
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&rect=260,390,312,407&color=example)
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=113,0,117,0&color=example)
> > because the [Divergence of Curl](1.2%20Differential%20Calculus.md#Divergence%20of%20Curl) is always zero, can you then determine the function F
> 
> 如何由这些已知条件求该函数？

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=177,7,184,1&color=note)
> > To solve a differential equation you must also **be supplied with appropriate boundary conditions**. In electrodynamics we typically require that the **fields go to zero “at infinity”** (far away from all charges).
> 
> > [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=239,0,243,32&color=example)
> >  In some textbook problems, the **charge itself extends to infinity** (we speak, for instance, of **the electric field of an infinite plane**, or the magnetic field of an infinite wire). In such cases the **normal boundary conditions do not apply**, and one must **invoke symmetry arguments** to determine the fields uniquely

> [!PDF|def] [01 Vector Analysis, p.51](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=51&selection=187,0,192,64&color=def)
> > With that extra information, the **Helmholtz theorem** guarantees that the field is **uniquely determined by its divergence and curl**
> 
> #thinkaboutit
