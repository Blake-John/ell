> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.24](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=24&selection=31,0,55,1&color=note)
> > In electrodynamics, we encounter several different kinds of integrals, among which the most important are **line (or path) integrals**, **surface integrals (or flux)**, and **volume integrals**.

> 
> > [!PDF|def] [01 Vector Analysis, p.24](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=24&selection=94,13,102,44&color=def)

> > At each point on the path, we take the **dot product of v (evaluated at that point) with the displacement dl to the next point** along the path. To a physicist, the most familiar example of **a line integral is the work done by a force** F: W = ∫ F · dl.

> [!PDF|important] [01 Vector Analysis, p.24](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=24&selection=145,0,167,2&color=important)
> > Ordinarily, the value of **a line integral depends critically on the path** taken from a to b, but there is **an important special class of vector functions** for which the line integral is independent of path and **is determined entirely by the end points**. It will be our business in due course to characterize this special class of vectors. (A force that has this property is said to be **conservative**.)

# 1.3.2 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.28](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=28&selection=155,0,168,9&color=note)
> > Suppose f (x) is a function of one variable. The **fundamental theorem of calculus** says that
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.28](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=28&rect=228,313,354,347&color=note)

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.28](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=28&selection=202,0,202,62&color=note)
> > In case this doesn’t look familiar, I’ll write it another way:
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.28](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=28&rect=230,259,349,291&color=note)
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.28](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=28&selection=232,0,268,1&color=note)
> > where df /dx = F (x). The fundamental theorem tells you how to integrate F (x): you think up a function f (x) whose derivative is equal to F.

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.28](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=28&selection=270,0,342,1&color=note)
> > Geometrical Interpretation: According to Eq. 1.33, df = (df /dx) dx is the infinitesimal change in f when you go from (x) to (x+dx). The fundamental theorem (Eq. 1.54) says that if you chop the interval from a to b (Fig. 1.25) into many tiny pieces, dx, and add up the increments df from each little piece, **the result is (not surprisingly) equal to the total change in f : f (b) − f (a)**.

> [!PDF|important] [01 Vector Analysis, p.28](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=28&selection=353,0,372,3&color=important)
> > Notice the structure of the fundamental theorem: **the integral of a derivative** over some region is **given by the value of the function at the end points** (boundaries). 

# 1.3.3 The Fundamental Theorem for Gradients

> [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.29](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=29&selection=51,0,79,24&color=example)
> > Suppose we have a scalar function of three variables T (x, y, z). Starting at point a, we move a small distance dl1 (Fig. 1.26). According to Eq. 1.37, the function T will change by an amount
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.29](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=29&rect=244,375,324,394&color=example)

> [!PDF|example] [01 Vector Analysis, p.29](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=29&selection=98,1,141,1&color=example)
> > Now we move a little farther, by an additional small displacement dl2 ; the incremental change in T will be (∇T ) · dl2 . In this manner, proceeding by infinitesimal steps, we **make the journey to point b**. At each step we compute the gradient of T (at that point) and dot it into the displacement dl … this **gives us the change in T**.

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.29](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=29&selection=141,2,157,28&color=note)
> > Evidently, the total change in T in going from a to b (along the path selected) is
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.29](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=29&rect=217,245,357,290&color=note)
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.29](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=29&selection=193,0,212,2&color=note)
> > This is the **fundamental theorem for gradients**; like the “ordinary” fundamental theorem, it says that the **integral (here a line integral) of a derivative (here the gradient) is given by the value of the function at the boundaries (a and b)**.
> 
> 只与边界有关

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.29](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=29&selection=239,47,244,64&color=note)
> >  Evidently, gradients have the special property that their line integrals are path independent:
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.30](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=30&selection=4,0,32,1&color=note)
> > Corollary 1. The integral ∫ b a (∇T ) · dl is independent of the path taken from a to b.
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.30](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=30&selection=34,0,72,2&color=note)
> > Corollary 2. The integral ∮ (∇T ) · dl = 0, since the beginning and end points are identical, and hence T (b) − T (a) = 0.
> 
> 梯度对路径的积分不依赖于具体路径，而取决于初末位置

> > The fundamental theorem for divergences states that
>

# 1.3.5 The Fundamental Theorem for Curls

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.33](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=33&selection=49,0,52,13&color=note)
> > The fundamental theorem for curls, which goes by the special name of **Stokes’ theorem**, states that
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.33](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=33&rect=224,367,351,410&color=note)

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.33](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=33&selection=86,11,115,1&color=note)
> > **the integral of a derivative** (here, the curl) **over a region** (here, a patch of surface, S) **is equal to the value of the function at the boundary** (here, the perimeter of the patch, P)

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.33](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=33&selection=119,0,138,12&color=note)
> > Geometrical Interpretation: Recall that the curl measures the “twist” of the vectors v; a region of high curl is a whirlpool – if you put a tiny paddle wheel there, it will rotate. Now, the integral of the curl over some surface (or, more precisely, the flux of the curl through that surface) represents the **“total amount of swirl,”** and we can determine that just as well by **going around the edge and finding how much the flow is following the boundary** (Fig. 1.31).
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.34](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=34&rect=157,571,306,653&color=note)

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.33](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=33&selection=138,12,156,1&color=note)
> >  Indeed, ∮ v · dl is sometimes called the **circulation** of v.
> 
> ![01 Vector Analysis, p.34](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=34&rect=322,573,428,663&color=note)
> 
> 换流量

> [!PDF|important] [01 Vector Analysis, p.33](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=33&selection=158,35,163,33&color=important)
> >  **which way are we supposed to go** around (clockwise or counterclockwise)? 
> 
> > [!PDF|important] [01 Vector Analysis, p.33](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=33&selection=190,28,197,12&color=important)
> >  Consistency in Stokes’ theorem (as in all such matters) is given by the **right-hand rule**: if your **fingers point in the direction of the line integral**, then your **thumb fixes the direction of da** (Fig. 1.32).
> 
> 

> [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.34](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=34&selection=25,21,44,74&color=note)
> > For Stokes’ theorem says that ∫ (∇×v)·da **is equal to the line integra**l of v around the boundary, and the latter **makes no reference to the specific surface** you choose.
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.34](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=34&selection=46,0,66,50&color=note)
> > Corollary 1. The integral ∫ (∇ × v) · da depends only on the boundary line, not on the particular surface used.
> 
> > [!PDF|note] [01 Vector Analysis, p.34](Physics/Electrodynamics/chapters/01%20Vector%20Analysis.pdf#page=34&selection=68,0,95,63&color=note)
> > Corollary 2. The integral ∮ (∇ × v) · da = 0 for any closed surface, since the boundary line, like the mouth of a balloon, shrinks down to a point, and hence the right-hand side of Eq. 1.57 vanishes.
